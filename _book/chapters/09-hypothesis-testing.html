20  Chapter 9: Hypothesis Testing

20.1 Introduction

Scientific progress depends on testing whether observed differences are genuine or merely due to chance. Hypothesis testing is the formal statistical framework for this investigation. In clinical trials, we test whether a new drug is truly better than placebo; in observational studies, we test whether an exposure is associated with an outcome. This chapter develops the logic of hypothesis testing, explains key concepts, and demonstrates essential tests for continuous and categorical data.

20.2 Part I: Foundations of Hypothesis Testing

20.2.1 The Null and Alternative Hypotheses

Every hypothesis test begins with two competing hypotheses:

  • Null hypothesis (\(H_0\)): Typically, a statement of “no effect” or “no difference.” For example, in a trial comparing a new antihypertensive to placebo, \(H_0\) might state that the mean blood pressure reduction is the same in both groups.
  • Alternative hypothesis (\(H_1\) or \(H_a\)): The research hypothesis; states that an effect or difference exists.

Hypotheses are formulated before analyzing data. The alternative hypothesis can be: - Two-tailed: Allows for differences in either direction (e.g., \(H_1: \mu_1 \neq \mu_2\)) - One-tailed: Specifies a direction (e.g., \(H_1: \mu_1 > \mu_2\), predicting drug is superior)

Two-tailed tests are more conservative and are generally preferred in clinical research unless there is strong prior reason to expect an effect only in one direction.

20.2.2 Type I and Type II Errors

In hypothesis testing, we make a decision (reject or fail to reject \(H_0\)) based on sample data. Because samples involve random variation, our decision may be wrong:

Decision \(H_0\) is True \(H_0\) is False
Reject \(H_0\) Type I Error (false positive, \(\alpha\)) Correct
Fail to Reject \(H_0\) Correct Type II Error (false negative, \(\beta\))
  • Type I error (significance level, \(\alpha\)): Rejecting \(H_0\) when it is actually true. Probability is set a priori, usually \(\alpha = 0.05\) (5% risk). A smaller \(\alpha\) reduces false positives but increases false negatives.
  • Type II error (\(\beta\)): Failing to reject \(H_0\) when \(H_1\) is actually true.
  • Power (\(1 - \beta\)): The probability of correctly detecting a true effect. Typically, we design studies to achieve power of 80% or 90%.

Clinical Example: A Type I error in a drug approval trial would mean approving an ineffective (or harmful) drug, exposing patients to unnecessary risk. A Type II error would mean rejecting a truly beneficial drug, denying patients access to effective treatment.

20.2.3 The p-value

The p-value is often misinterpreted. Here is the correct definition:

\[\text{p-value} = P(\text{observed data or more extreme} \mid H_0 \text{ is true})\]

It is the probability of obtaining data as extreme (or more extreme) as those observed, assuming the null hypothesis is true.

Correct interpretation: A p-value of 0.03 means: “If the null hypothesis were true, we would observe a test statistic as extreme as (or more extreme than) the one calculated from our data 3% of the time.”

Incorrect interpretation: “The probability that the null hypothesis is true is 0.03” or “The probability the drug works is 0.97.” The p-value says nothing about the posterior probability of the null hypothesis.

Decision Rule: - If p-value \(< \alpha\) (e.g., \(< 0.05\)): Reject \(H_0\); declare the result statistically significant. - If p-value \(\geq \alpha\): Fail to reject \(H_0\); insufficient evidence to reject the null.

Note: A non-significant result does not prove \(H_0\) is true; it merely indicates insufficient evidence to reject it.

20.3 Part II: One-Sample Tests

20.3.1 One-Sample t-Test

The one-sample t-test determines whether the mean of a single sample differs significantly from a hypothesized value \(\mu_0\).

Hypotheses: \[H_0: \mu = \mu_0\] \[H_1: \mu \neq \mu_0 \text{ (two-tailed)}\]

Test Statistic: \[t = \frac{\bar{x} - \mu_0}{s/\sqrt{n}}\]

where \(\bar{x}\) is the sample mean, \(s\) is the sample SD, and \(n\) is the sample size. Under \(H_0\), \(t\) follows a t-distribution with \(n-1\) degrees of freedom.

Assumptions: 1. Independence: Observations are independent of one another. 2. Normality: Data come from a normal distribution (or the sample is large by Central Limit Theorem, \(n \geq 30\)).

Clinical Example: A clinical team measures fasting glucose in 15 patients with newly diagnosed diabetes. Previous studies indicate that untreated fasting glucose averages 150 mg/dL in this population. The team observes a sample mean of 162 mg/dL (SD = 25). Does this sample differ significantly from the historical control?

\[t = \frac{162 - 150}{25/\sqrt{15}} = \frac{12}{6.45} = 1.86\]

With df = 14, the two-tailed p-value is approximately 0.081, which exceeds \(\alpha = 0.05\). Thus, the sample mean does not differ significantly from the historical control at the 5% level (though the result is suggestive).

20.3.2 One-Sample Wilcoxon Signed-Rank Test

For non-normally distributed data or small samples, the Wilcoxon signed-rank test is a non-parametric alternative to the t-test. It tests the median rather than the mean.

Test Procedure: 1. Compute deviations: \(d_i = x_i - \mu_0\) 2. Rank absolute deviations: \(|d_i|\), ignoring zeros 3. Assign signs based on the sign of \(d_i\) 4. Compute the test statistic \(W\) = sum of ranks with the less frequent sign

The distribution of \(W\) under \(H_0\) is known, and p-values are obtained from tables or software.

Advantages: - Robust to outliers and non-normality - Based on ranks, not raw values - Suitable for small samples

Clinical Use: Ideal for skewed data (e.g., time to recovery, biomarker concentrations) or when normality is suspect.

20.4 Part III: Two-Sample Tests

20.4.1 Two-Sample Independent t-Test

The two-sample t-test compares means between two independent groups.

Hypotheses: \[H_0: \mu_1 = \mu_2\] \[H_1: \mu_1 \neq \mu_2\]

Test Statistic (Equal Variance Case):

Assuming equal variances (verified by Levene’s test), the pooled standard error is:

\[s_p^2 = \frac{(n_1 - 1)s_1^2 + (n_2 - 1)s_2^2}{n_1 + n_2 - 2}\]

The t-statistic is:

\[t = \frac{\bar{x}_1 - \bar{x}_2}{\sqrt{s_p^2(1/n_1 + 1/n_2)}}\]

Under \(H_0\), \(t\) follows a t-distribution with \(n_1 + n_2 - 2\) degrees of freedom.

Assumptions: 1. Independence: Observations within and between groups are independent. 2. Normality: Both groups are normally distributed (or \(n \geq 30\)). 3. Homogeneity of Variance: The variances are equal in both groups, i.e., \(\sigma_1^2 = \sigma_2^2\).

Testing Homogeneity: Levene’s test is robust to departures from normality: \[H_0: \sigma_1^2 = \sigma_2^2\]

If Levene’s test rejects (p < 0.05), use Welch’s t-test (below).

Clinical Example: A randomized trial compares mean blood pressure reduction (mmHg) between an ACE inhibitor (Group 1) and placebo (Group 2) after 12 weeks: - Group 1 (ACE inhibitor): \(n_1 = 50\), \(\bar{x}_1 = 18.5\) mmHg, \(s_1 = 8.2\) mmHg - Group 2 (Placebo): \(n_2 = 50\), \(\bar{x}_2 = 4.2\) mmHg, \(s_2 = 7.9\) mmHg

Pooled variance: \(s_p^2 = \frac{49(8.2)^2 + 49(7.9)^2}{98} = 64.95\)

\[t = \frac{18.5 - 4.2}{\sqrt{64.95 \cdot (1/50 + 1/50)}} = \frac{14.3}{1.61} = 8.88\]

With df = 98, the two-tailed p-value is < 0.0001. The ACE inhibitor produces significantly greater blood pressure reduction than placebo.

20.4.2 Welch’s t-Test

When variances are unequal, Welch’s t-test provides a more reliable test statistic:

\[t = \frac{\bar{x}_1 - \bar{x}_2}{\sqrt{s_1^2/n_1 + s_2^2/n_2}}\]

The degrees of freedom are estimated using the Welch-Satterthwaite equation, which typically yields a non-integer value. Most statistical software handles this automatically.

Advantage: More robust to unequal variances; recommended as a default for two-sample comparisons.

20.4.3 Mann-Whitney U Test

The Mann-Whitney U test is the non-parametric alternative to the two-sample t-test. It tests whether two distributions differ in location (comparing medians).

Test Procedure: 1. Rank all observations from both groups together (1 to \(n_1 + n_2\)) 2. Compute \(U_1\) = number of times a value in group 1 precedes a value in group 2 3. Alternatively: \(U_1 = n_1 n_2 + \frac{n_1(n_1+1)}{2} - R_1\), where \(R_1\) is the sum of ranks in group 1

The test statistic is the smaller of \(U_1\) and \(U_2 = n_1 n_2 - U_1\). The p-value is obtained from the null distribution of \(U\) (tabulated for small samples; approximates normal for large samples).

Advantages: - Non-parametric; no distributional assumptions - Robust to outliers - More powerful than t-test for skewed data

Clinical Use: Ideal for skewed or ordinal data (e.g., symptom severity scores, hospital length of stay).

20.4.4 Paired t-Test

When observations are paired (e.g., before-and-after measurements in the same patient), use the paired t-test. This design reduces variability by eliminating between-patient differences.

Setup: Compute differences: \(d_i = x_{i,\text{before}} - x_{i,\text{after}}\) (or vice versa) for each pair.

Hypotheses: \[H_0: \mu_d = 0\] \[H_1: \mu_d \neq 0\]

Test Statistic: \[t = \frac{\bar{d}}{s_d/\sqrt{n}}\]

where \(\bar{d}\) is the mean difference and \(s_d\) is the SD of differences. Under \(H_0\), \(t\) follows a t-distribution with \(n-1\) degrees of freedom.

Assumptions: 1. Pairing: Observations are naturally paired or matched. 2. Independence: Pairs are independent of one another. 3. Normality: Differences are approximately normally distributed.

Clinical Example (sleep dataset): The classic datasets::sleep dataset contains extra sleep hours gained under two soporific drugs in 10 patients (crossover design, each patient received both drugs):

Patient    Drug 1    Drug 2    Difference (Drug 2 - Drug 1)
1          0.7       1.9       1.2
2         -1.6       0.8       2.4
...

We test \(H_0: \mu_d = 0\) (no difference between drugs) vs. \(H_1: \mu_d \neq 0\). The paired t-test accounts for the fact that some patients naturally sleep longer than others, removing this source of variability.

20.5 Part IV: Tests for Categorical Data

20.5.1 Chi-Square Test of Independence

The chi-square test assesses whether two categorical variables are independent.

Hypotheses: \[H_0: \text{Variables are independent}\] \[H_1: \text{Variables are associated}\]

Test Statistic: \[\chi^2 = \sum_{i,j} \frac{(O_{ij} - E_{ij})^2}{E_{ij}}\]

where \(O_{ij}\) is the observed count in cell \((i,j)\) and \(E_{ij}\) is the expected count under independence:

\[E_{ij} = \frac{(\text{Row } i \text{ Total}) \times (\text{Column } j \text{ Total})}{\text{Grand Total}}\]

Under \(H_0\), \(\chi^2\) approximately follows a chi-square distribution with \((\text{rows} - 1) \times (\text{columns} - 1)\) degrees of freedom.

Assumptions: 1. Independence: Observations are independent. 2. Expected Counts: Expected counts should be \(\geq 5\) in at least 80% of cells (otherwise, use Fisher’s exact test).

Clinical Example: A phase IV safety study compares the incidence of myocardial infarction (MI) in users of two NSAIDs:

Drug MI No MI Total
Drug A 45 955 1000
Drug B 28 972 1000
Total 73 1927 2000

Expected counts: \(E_{11} = \frac{73 \times 1000}{2000} = 36.5\), and so on.

\[\chi^2 = \frac{(45-36.5)^2}{36.5} + \frac{(955-963.5)^2}{963.5} + \cdots = 2.91\]

With df = 1, the one-tailed p-value \(\approx 0.088\). At \(\alpha = 0.05\), we fail to reject independence; the association is not statistically significant.

20.5.2 Fisher’s Exact Test

When expected counts are small (< 5), Fisher’s exact test provides an exact p-value without distributional assumptions. It is particularly useful for small samples or rare events.

Procedure: Condition on the marginal totals and compute the probability of the observed table and all more extreme tables under the assumption of independence. Sum these probabilities to obtain the p-value.

Computational Note: Modern software computes Fisher’s exact test via the hypergeometric distribution. The test is more computationally intensive but more accurate than chi-square for small samples.

Clinical Use: Ideal for comparing the incidence of rare adverse events between treatment groups in small trials.

20.6 Part V: Multiple Testing and Type I Error Control

When conducting multiple hypothesis tests, the probability of at least one Type I error increases. If we perform \(m\) independent tests each at \(\alpha = 0.05\), the probability of at least one false positive is:

\[P(\text{at least one Type I error}) = 1 - (1 - \alpha)^m\]

For example, with 10 tests: \(1 - (0.95)^{10} \approx 0.40\) (40% risk of at least one false positive).

20.6.1 Bonferroni Correction

The Bonferroni correction divides the significance level by the number of tests:

\[\alpha_{\text{Bonferroni}} = \frac{\alpha}{m}\]

If conducting 10 tests at the conventional \(\alpha = 0.05\), each individual test would be evaluated at \(\alpha = 0.005\). This is conservative; it controls the family-wise error rate (FWER) at 0.05.

Advantage: Simple and interpretable. Disadvantage: Very conservative; may lack power for detecting true effects.

20.6.2 Benjamini-Hochberg (BH) Correction

The Benjamini-Hochberg procedure controls the false discovery rate (FDR)—the expected proportion of false positives among rejected hypotheses. This is less conservative than Bonferroni.

Procedure: 1. Rank p-values from smallest to largest: \(p_{(1)} \leq p_{(2)} \leq \cdots \leq p_{(m)}\) 2. Find the largest \(i\) such that \(p_{(i)} \leq \frac{i}{m} \alpha\) 3. Reject all hypotheses \(H_0^{(1)}, \ldots, H_0^{(i)}\)

Advantage: More powerful than Bonferroni while controlling FDR. Disadvantage: More complex; requires understanding of FDR.

Clinical Example: In a genomics study, researchers test 20,000 genes for differential expression between two treatment groups. Using Bonferroni, the threshold would be \(p < 0.05/20000 = 2.5 \times 10^{-6}\)—extremely stringent. The BH procedure would be more powerful while still controlling false positives in expectation.

20.7 Part VI: Practical Implementation in R

20.7.1 One-Sample Tests

# Load data
data(sleep)
head(sleep)

# One-sample t-test: Does extra sleep differ from 0?
t.test(sleep$extra, mu = 0)

# Output interpretation:
#   t = 4.06, df = 9, p-value = 0.002
#   95% CI: [1.0, 2.8] hours
#   Interpretation: Extra sleep is significantly greater than 0.

20.7.2 Paired t-Test (sleep dataset)

# Reshape data for paired analysis
sleep_wide <- sleep %>%
  pivot_wider(
    id_cols = ID,
    names_from = group,
    values_from = extra
  )

# Compute differences
sleep_wide$diff <- sleep_wide$`2` - sleep_wide$`1`

# Paired t-test
t.test(sleep_wide$`2`, sleep_wide$`1`, paired = TRUE)

# Alternative: directly on differences
t.test(sleep_wide$diff, mu = 0)

# Output:
#   t = 4.06, df = 9, p-value = 0.002
#   95% CI: [0.7, 2.3] hours
#   Interpretation: Drug 2 yields significantly more extra sleep than Drug 1.

# Wilcoxon signed-rank test (non-parametric alternative)
wilcox.test(sleep_wide$`2`, sleep_wide$`1`, paired = TRUE)

20.7.3 Two-Sample Independent Tests

# Using PlantGrowth as a clinical example
data(PlantGrowth)

# Compare "ctrl" vs "trt1" (two groups)
group_ctrl <- PlantGrowth$weight[PlantGrowth$group == "ctrl"]
group_trt1 <- PlantGrowth$weight[PlantGrowth$group == "trt1"]

# Check equality of variances using Levene's test
library(car)
leveneTest(weight ~ group, data = PlantGrowth)

# Two-sample t-test (assuming equal variances)
t.test(group_ctrl, group_trt1, var.equal = TRUE)

# Welch's t-test (does not assume equal variances; safer default)
t.test(group_ctrl, group_trt1, var.equal = FALSE)

# Mann-Whitney U test (non-parametric)
wilcox.test(group_ctrl, group_trt1)

20.7.4 Pharmaversesdtm Example: Comparing SBP Between Treatment Arms

library(pharmaversesdtm)
library(dplyr)

data(dm)
data(vs)

# Prepare data: baseline SBP by treatment arm
sbp_data <- vs %>%
  filter(VSTESTCD == "SYSBP" & VISIT == "BASELINE") %>%
  select(USUBJID, VSSTRESN) %>%
  left_join(dm %>% select(USUBJID, ARM), by = "USUBJID") %>%
  rename(SBP = VSSTRESN) %>%
  na.omit()

# Summary by group
sbp_data %>%
  group_by(ARM) %>%
  summarise(
    N = n(),
    Mean = mean(SBP),
    SD = sd(SBP),
    Median = median(SBP)
  )

# Filter to 2-arm comparison for t-test (requires exactly 2 levels)
sbp_2arm <- sbp_data %>%
  filter(ARM %in% c("Placebo", "Xanomeline High Dose"))

# Two-sample t-test
t_test_result <- t.test(
  SBP ~ ARM,
  data = sbp_2arm,
  var.equal = FALSE
)
print(t_test_result)

# Mann-Whitney U test (if non-normal)
wilcox.test(SBP ~ ARM, data = sbp_2arm)

20.7.5 Chi-Square and Fisher’s Exact Tests

# Simulated adverse event data
ae_data <- data.frame(
  Treatment = c("Drug A", "Drug A", "Drug B", "Drug B"),
  AE = c("Yes", "No", "Yes", "No"),
  Count = c(12, 138, 8, 142)
)

# Create contingency table
ae_table <- xtabs(Count ~ Treatment + AE, data = ae_data)
print(ae_table)

# Chi-square test
chisq_result <- chisq.test(ae_table)
print(chisq_result)

# Fisher's exact test (more appropriate for small expected counts)
fisher.test(ae_table)

# Adverse events from pharmaversesdtm
library(pharmaversesdtm)
data(ae)
data(dm)

# Incidence of any adverse event by treatment
ae_incidence <- ae %>%
  select(USUBJID) %>%
  distinct() %>%
  mutate(AE = "Yes") %>%
  right_join(
    dm %>% select(USUBJID, ARM),
    by = "USUBJID"
  ) %>%
  mutate(AE = ifelse(is.na(AE), "No", "Yes"))

# Create contingency table
ae_table_pharm <- table(ae_incidence$ARM, ae_incidence$AE)
print(ae_table_pharm)

# Chi-square test
chisq.test(ae_table_pharm)

20.7.6 Multiple Testing Correction

# Example: Testing 10 laboratory parameters for differences between groups
set.seed(42)
p_values <- c(0.001, 0.008, 0.015, 0.023, 0.045, 0.067, 0.089, 0.12, 0.34, 0.89)

# Bonferroni correction
bonferroni_threshold <- 0.05 / length(p_values)
bonferroni_adjusted <- p_values * length(p_values)
bonferroni_significant <- p_values < bonferroni_threshold

cat("Bonferroni threshold:", bonferroni_threshold, "\n")
cat("Number significant:", sum(bonferroni_significant), "\n")

# Benjamini-Hochberg correction
bh_result <- p.adjust(p_values, method = "BH")
bh_significant <- bh_result < 0.05

cat("\nBenjamini-Hochberg method:\n")
print(data.frame(p_value = p_values, p_adj = bh_result, significant = bh_significant))

# Comparison
comparison <- data.frame(
  Test = paste0("Param ", 1:10),
  Raw_p = p_values,
  Bonferroni = bonferroni_adjusted,
  BH = bh_result,
  Bonf_Sig = bonferroni_significant,
  BH_Sig = bh_significant
)
print(comparison)

Scientific progress depends on testing whether observed differences are genuine or merely due to chance. Hypothesis testing is the formal statistical framework for this investigation. In clinical trials, we test whether a new drug is truly better than placebo; in observational studies, we test whether an exposure is associated with an outcome. ## Part I: Foundations of Hypothesis Testing

### The Null and Alternative Hypotheses

Every hypothesis test begins with two competing hypotheses:

- **Null hypothesis ($H_0$)**: Typically, a statement of "no effect" or "no difference." For example, in a trial comparing a new antihypertensive to placebo, $H_0$ might state that the mean blood pressure reduction is the same in both groups.
- **Alternative hypothesis ($H_1$ or $H_a$)**: The research hypothesis; states that an effect or difference exists.

Hypotheses are formulated *before* analyzing data. The alternative hypothesis can be:
- **Two-tailed**: Allows for differences in either direction (e.g., $H_1: \mu_1 \neq \mu_2$)
- **One-tailed**: Specifies a direction (e.g., $H_1: \mu_1 > \mu_2$, predicting drug is superior)

Two-tailed tests are more conservative and are generally preferred in clinical research unless there is strong prior reason to expect an effect only in one direction.

### Type I and Type II Errors

In hypothesis testing, we make a decision (reject or fail to reject $H_0$) based on sample data. Because samples involve random variation, our decision may be wrong:

| Decision | $H_0$ is True | $H_0$ is False |
|----------|---------------|----------------|
| Reject $H_0$ | **Type I Error** (false positive, $\alpha$) | Correct |
| Fail to Reject $H_0$ | Correct | **Type II Error** (false negative, $\beta$) |

- **Type I error (significance level, $\alpha$)**: Rejecting $H_0$ when it is actually true. Probability is set *a priori*, usually $\alpha = 0.05$ (5% risk). A smaller $\alpha$ reduces false positives but increases false negatives.
- **Type II error ($\beta$)**: Failing to reject $H_0$ when $H_1$ is actually true.
- **Power ($1 - \beta$)**: The probability of correctly detecting a true effect. Typically, we design studies to achieve power of 80% or 90%.

**Clinical Example:** A Type I error in a drug approval trial would mean approving an ineffective (or harmful) drug, exposing patients to unnecessary risk. A Type II error would mean rejecting a truly beneficial drug, denying patients access to effective treatment.

### The p-value

The **p-value** is often misinterpreted. Here is the correct definition:

$$\text{p-value} = P(\text{observed data or more extreme} \mid H_0 \text{ is true})$$

It is the probability of obtaining data as extreme (or more extreme) as those observed, *assuming the null hypothesis is true*.

**Correct interpretation:** A p-value of 0.03 means: "If the null hypothesis were true, we would observe a test statistic as extreme as (or more extreme than) the one calculated from our data 3% of the time."

**Incorrect interpretation:** "The probability that the null hypothesis is true is 0.03" or "The probability the drug works is 0.97." The p-value says nothing about the posterior probability of the null hypothesis.

**Decision Rule:**
- If p-value $< \alpha$ (e.g., $< 0.05$): Reject $H_0$; declare the result statistically significant.
- If p-value $\geq \alpha$: Fail to reject $H_0$; insufficient evidence to reject the null.

Note: A non-significant result does not prove $H_0$ is true; it merely indicates insufficient evidence to reject it.

## Part II: One-Sample Tests

### One-Sample t-Test

The one-sample t-test determines whether the mean of a single sample differs significantly from a hypothesized value $\mu_0$.

**Hypotheses:**
$$H_0: \mu = \mu_0$$
$$H_1: \mu \neq \mu_0 \text{ (two-tailed)}$$

**Test Statistic:**
$$t = \frac{\bar{x} - \mu_0}{s/\sqrt{n}}$$

where $\bar{x}$ is the sample mean, $s$ is the sample SD, and $n$ is the sample size. Under $H_0$, $t$ follows a t-distribution with $n-1$ degrees of freedom.

**Assumptions:**
1. **Independence**: Observations are independent of one another.
2. **Normality**: Data come from a normal distribution (or the sample is large by Central Limit Theorem, $n \geq 30$).

**Clinical Example:** A clinical team measures fasting glucose in 15 patients with newly diagnosed diabetes. Previous studies indicate that untreated fasting glucose averages 150 mg/dL in this population. The team observes a sample mean of 162 mg/dL (SD = 25). Does this sample differ significantly from the historical control?

$$t = \frac{162 - 150}{25/\sqrt{15}} = \frac{12}{6.45} = 1.86$$

With df = 14, the two-tailed p-value is approximately 0.081, which exceeds $\alpha = 0.05$. Thus, the sample mean does not differ significantly from the historical control at the 5% level (though the result is suggestive).

### One-Sample Wilcoxon Signed-Rank Test

For non-normally distributed data or small samples, the **Wilcoxon signed-rank test** is a non-parametric alternative to the t-test. It tests the median rather than the mean.

**Test Procedure:**
1. Compute deviations: $d_i = x_i - \mu_0$
2. Rank absolute deviations: $|d_i|$, ignoring zeros
3. Assign signs based on the sign of $d_i$
4. Compute the test statistic $W$ = sum of ranks with the less frequent sign

The distribution of $W$ under $H_0$ is known, and p-values are obtained from tables or software.

**Advantages:**
- Robust to outliers and non-normality
- Based on ranks, not raw values
- Suitable for small samples

**Clinical Use:** Ideal for skewed data (e.g., time to recovery, biomarker concentrations) or when normality is suspect.

## Part III: Two-Sample Tests

### Two-Sample Independent t-Test

The two-sample t-test compares means between two independent groups.

**Hypotheses:**
$$H_0: \mu_1 = \mu_2$$
$$H_1: \mu_1 \neq \mu_2$$

**Test Statistic (Equal Variance Case):**

Assuming equal variances (verified by Levene's test), the pooled standard error is:

$$s_p^2 = \frac{(n_1 - 1)s_1^2 + (n_2 - 1)s_2^2}{n_1 + n_2 - 2}$$

The t-statistic is:

$$t = \frac{\bar{x}_1 - \bar{x}_2}{\sqrt{s_p^2(1/n_1 + 1/n_2)}}$$

Under $H_0$, $t$ follows a t-distribution with $n_1 + n_2 - 2$ degrees of freedom.

**Assumptions:**
1. **Independence**: Observations within and between groups are independent.
2. **Normality**: Both groups are normally distributed (or $n \geq 30$).
3. **Homogeneity of Variance**: The variances are equal in both groups, i.e., $\sigma_1^2 = \sigma_2^2$.

**Testing Homogeneity:** Levene's test is robust to departures from normality:
$$H_0: \sigma_1^2 = \sigma_2^2$$

If Levene's test rejects (p < 0.05), use **Welch's t-test** (below).

**Clinical Example:** A randomized trial compares mean blood pressure reduction (mmHg) between an ACE inhibitor (Group 1) and placebo (Group 2) after 12 weeks:
- Group 1 (ACE inhibitor): $n_1 = 50$, $\bar{x}_1 = 18.5$ mmHg, $s_1 = 8.2$ mmHg
- Group 2 (Placebo): $n_2 = 50$, $\bar{x}_2 = 4.2$ mmHg, $s_2 = 7.9$ mmHg

Pooled variance: $s_p^2 = \frac{49(8.2)^2 + 49(7.9)^2}{98} = 64.95$

$$t = \frac{18.5 - 4.2}{\sqrt{64.95 \cdot (1/50 + 1/50)}} = \frac{14.3}{1.61} = 8.88$$

With df = 98, the two-tailed p-value is < 0.0001. The ACE inhibitor produces significantly greater blood pressure reduction than placebo.

### Welch's t-Test

When variances are unequal, **Welch's t-test** provides a more reliable test statistic:

$$t = \frac{\bar{x}_1 - \bar{x}_2}{\sqrt{s_1^2/n_1 + s_2^2/n_2}}$$

The degrees of freedom are estimated using the Welch-Satterthwaite equation, which typically yields a non-integer value. Most statistical software handles this automatically.

**Advantage:** More robust to unequal variances; recommended as a default for two-sample comparisons.

### Mann-Whitney U Test

The **Mann-Whitney U test** is the non-parametric alternative to the two-sample t-test. It tests whether two distributions differ in location (comparing medians).

**Test Procedure:**
1. Rank all observations from both groups together (1 to $n_1 + n_2$)
2. Compute $U_1$ = number of times a value in group 1 precedes a value in group 2
3. Alternatively: $U_1 = n_1 n_2 + \frac{n_1(n_1+1)}{2} - R_1$, where $R_1$ is the sum of ranks in group 1

The test statistic is the smaller of $U_1$ and $U_2 = n_1 n_2 - U_1$. The p-value is obtained from the null distribution of $U$ (tabulated for small samples; approximates normal for large samples).

**Advantages:**
- Non-parametric; no distributional assumptions
- Robust to outliers
- More powerful than t-test for skewed data

**Clinical Use:** Ideal for skewed or ordinal data (e.g., symptom severity scores, hospital length of stay).

### Paired t-Test

When observations are **paired** (e.g., before-and-after measurements in the same patient), use the paired t-test. This design reduces variability by eliminating between-patient differences.

**Setup:** Compute differences: $d_i = x_{i,\text{before}} - x_{i,\text{after}}$ (or vice versa) for each pair.

**Hypotheses:**
$$H_0: \mu_d = 0$$
$$H_1: \mu_d \neq 0$$

**Test Statistic:**
$$t = \frac{\bar{d}}{s_d/\sqrt{n}}$$

where $\bar{d}$ is the mean difference and $s_d$ is the SD of differences. Under $H_0$, $t$ follows a t-distribution with $n-1$ degrees of freedom.

**Assumptions:**
1. **Pairing**: Observations are naturally paired or matched.
2. **Independence**: Pairs are independent of one another.
3. **Normality**: Differences are approximately normally distributed.

**Clinical Example (sleep dataset):** The classic `datasets::sleep` dataset contains extra sleep hours gained under two soporific drugs in 10 patients (crossover design, each patient received both drugs):

```
Patient    Drug 1    Drug 2    Difference (Drug 2 - Drug 1)
1          0.7       1.9       1.2
2         -1.6       0.8       2.4
...
```

We test $H_0: \mu_d = 0$ (no difference between drugs) vs. $H_1: \mu_d \neq 0$. The paired t-test accounts for the fact that some patients naturally sleep longer than others, removing this source of variability.

## Part IV: Tests for Categorical Data

### Chi-Square Test of Independence

The chi-square test assesses whether two categorical variables are independent.

**Hypotheses:**
$$H_0: \text{Variables are independent}$$
$$H_1: \text{Variables are associated}$$

**Test Statistic:**
$$\chi^2 = \sum_{i,j} \frac{(O_{ij} - E_{ij})^2}{E_{ij}}$$

where $O_{ij}$ is the observed count in cell $(i,j)$ and $E_{ij}$ is the expected count under independence:

$$E_{ij} = \frac{(\text{Row } i \text{ Total}) \times (\text{Column } j \text{ Total})}{\text{Grand Total}}$$

Under $H_0$, $\chi^2$ approximately follows a chi-square distribution with $(\text{rows} - 1) \times (\text{columns} - 1)$ degrees of freedom.

**Assumptions:**
1. **Independence**: Observations are independent.
2. **Expected Counts**: Expected counts should be $\geq 5$ in at least 80% of cells (otherwise, use Fisher's exact test).

**Clinical Example:** A phase IV safety study compares the incidence of myocardial infarction (MI) in users of two NSAIDs:

| Drug | MI | No MI | Total |
|------|-------|---------|-------|
| Drug A | 45 | 955 | 1000 |
| Drug B | 28 | 972 | 1000 |
| Total | 73 | 1927 | 2000 |

Expected counts: $E_{11} = \frac{73 \times 1000}{2000} = 36.5$, and so on.

$$\chi^2 = \frac{(45-36.5)^2}{36.5} + \frac{(955-963.5)^2}{963.5} + \cdots = 2.91$$

With df = 1, the one-tailed p-value $\approx 0.088$. At $\alpha = 0.05$, we fail to reject independence; the association is not statistically significant.

### Fisher's Exact Test

When expected counts are small (< 5), **Fisher's exact test** provides an exact p-value without distributional assumptions. It is particularly useful for small samples or rare events.

**Procedure:** Condition on the marginal totals and compute the probability of the observed table and all more extreme tables under the assumption of independence. Sum these probabilities to obtain the p-value.

**Computational Note:** Modern software computes Fisher's exact test via the hypergeometric distribution. The test is more computationally intensive but more accurate than chi-square for small samples.

**Clinical Use:** Ideal for comparing the incidence of rare adverse events between treatment groups in small trials.

## Part V: Multiple Testing and Type I Error Control

When conducting multiple hypothesis tests, the probability of at least one Type I error increases. If we perform $m$ independent tests each at $\alpha = 0.05$, the probability of at least one false positive is:

$$P(\text{at least one Type I error}) = 1 - (1 - \alpha)^m$$

For example, with 10 tests: $1 - (0.95)^{10} \approx 0.40$ (40% risk of at least one false positive).

### Bonferroni Correction

The **Bonferroni correction** divides the significance level by the number of tests:

$$\alpha_{\text{Bonferroni}} = \frac{\alpha}{m}$$

If conducting 10 tests at the conventional $\alpha = 0.05$, each individual test would be evaluated at $\alpha = 0.005$. This is conservative; it controls the **family-wise error rate (FWER)** at 0.05.

**Advantage:** Simple and interpretable.
**Disadvantage:** Very conservative; may lack power for detecting true effects.

### Benjamini-Hochberg (BH) Correction

The **Benjamini-Hochberg procedure** controls the **false discovery rate (FDR)**—the expected proportion of false positives among rejected hypotheses. This is less conservative than Bonferroni.

**Procedure:**
1. Rank p-values from smallest to largest: $p_{(1)} \leq p_{(2)} \leq \cdots \leq p_{(m)}$
2. Find the largest $i$ such that $p_{(i)} \leq \frac{i}{m} \alpha$
3. Reject all hypotheses $H_0^{(1)}, \ldots, H_0^{(i)}$

**Advantage:** More powerful than Bonferroni while controlling FDR.
**Disadvantage:** More complex; requires understanding of FDR.

**Clinical Example:** In a genomics study, researchers test 20,000 genes for differential expression between two treatment groups. Using Bonferroni, the threshold would be $p < 0.05/20000 = 2.5 \times 10^{-6}$—extremely stringent. The BH procedure would be more powerful while still controlling false positives in expectation.

## Part VI: Practical Implementation in R

### One-Sample Tests

```r
# Load data
data(sleep)
head(sleep)

# One-sample t-test: Does extra sleep differ from 0?
t.test(sleep$extra, mu = 0)

# Output interpretation:
#   t = 4.06, df = 9, p-value = 0.002
#   95% CI: [1.0, 2.8] hours
#   Interpretation: Extra sleep is significantly greater than 0.
```

### Paired t-Test (sleep dataset)

```r
# Reshape data for paired analysis
sleep_wide <- sleep %>%
  pivot_wider(
    id_cols = ID,
    names_from = group,
    values_from = extra
  )

# Compute differences
sleep_wide$diff <- sleep_wide$`2` - sleep_wide$`1`

# Paired t-test
t.test(sleep_wide$`2`, sleep_wide$`1`, paired = TRUE)

# Alternative: directly on differences
t.test(sleep_wide$diff, mu = 0)

# Output:
#   t = 4.06, df = 9, p-value = 0.002
#   95% CI: [0.7, 2.3] hours
#   Interpretation: Drug 2 yields significantly more extra sleep than Drug 1.

# Wilcoxon signed-rank test (non-parametric alternative)
wilcox.test(sleep_wide$`2`, sleep_wide$`1`, paired = TRUE)
```

### Two-Sample Independent Tests

```r
# Using PlantGrowth as a clinical example
data(PlantGrowth)

# Compare "ctrl" vs "trt1" (two groups)
group_ctrl <- PlantGrowth$weight[PlantGrowth$group == "ctrl"]
group_trt1 <- PlantGrowth$weight[PlantGrowth$group == "trt1"]

# Check equality of variances using Levene's test
library(car)
leveneTest(weight ~ group, data = PlantGrowth)

# Two-sample t-test (assuming equal variances)
t.test(group_ctrl, group_trt1, var.equal = TRUE)

# Welch's t-test (does not assume equal variances; safer default)

# Mann-Whitney U test (non-parametric)
wilcox.test(group_ctrl, group_trt1)
```

ae_data <- data.frame(

### Multiple Testing Correction

```r
# Example: Testing 10 laboratory parameters for differences between groups
set.seed(42)
p_values <- c(0.001, 0.008, 0.015, 0.023, 0.045, 0.067, 0.089, 0.12, 0.34, 0.89)

# Bonferroni correction
bonferroni_threshold <- 0.05 / length(p_values)
bonferroni_adjusted <- p_values * length(p_values)
bonferroni_significant <- p_values < bonferroni_threshold

cat("Bonferroni threshold:", bonferroni_threshold, "\n")
cat("Number significant:", sum(bonferroni_significant), "\n")

# Benjamini-Hochberg correction
bh_result <- p.adjust(p_values, method = "BH")
bh_significant <- bh_result < 0.05

cat("\nBenjamini-Hochberg method:\n")
print(data.frame(p_value = p_values, p_adj = bh_result, significant = bh_significant))

# Comparison
comparison <- data.frame(
  Test = paste0("Param ", 1:10),
  Raw_p = p_values,
  Bonferroni = bonferroni_adjusted,
  BH = bh_result,
  Bonf_Sig = bonferroni_significant,
  BH_Sig = bh_significant
)
print(comparison)
```

## Summary

| Test | Purpose | Assumption | R Function |
|------|---------|-----------|-----------|
| **One-sample t-test** | Compare sample mean to constant | Normality | `t.test(x, mu = value)` |
| **Wilcoxon signed-rank** | Non-parametric version | Symmetry | `wilcox.test(x, mu = value)` |
| **Paired t-test** | Compare paired measurements | Normality of differences | `t.test(x, y, paired = TRUE)` |
| **Two-sample t-test** | Compare two group means | Normality, equal variance | `t.test(x ~ group)` |
| **Welch's t-test** | Two-sample, unequal variance | Normality | `t.test(x ~ group, var.equal = FALSE)` |
| **Mann-Whitney U** | Non-parametric two-sample | — | `wilcox.test(x ~ group)` |
| **Chi-square** | Test categorical association | Expected counts ≥ 5 | `chisq.test()` |
| **Fisher's exact** | Rare events / small counts | — | `fisher.test()` |
