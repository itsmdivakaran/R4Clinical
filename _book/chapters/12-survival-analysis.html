23  Survival Analysis

23.1 Introduction: What is Survival Data?

Survival analysis is a cornerstone of clinical research, particularly in oncology, cardiology, and infectious disease trials. Unlike traditional statistical methods that assume all observations are complete, survival analysis handles time-to-event data where some patients may not experience the event of interest by the end of the study period. This incomplete information is called censoring, and ignoring it can lead to biased estimates and incorrect conclusions.

In a typical clinical trial: - Event: patient death, disease recurrence, disease progression, or any clinically relevant endpoint - Time: duration from study enrollment to event occurrence or censoring - Censoring: when we know the patient was event-free up to a certain time, but don’t know their status afterwards

23.1.1 Why Survival Analysis Matters in Clinical Practice

Consider a lung cancer trial. At the data cutoff date (e.g., 18 months post-enrollment), some patients have died (event observed) while others are still alive. We cannot simply record those alive patients as “no event” because they may die later. Instead, we censor them—acknowledging that their true survival time is at least as long as their follow-up time.

23.2 Types of Censoring

23.2.1 Right Censoring (Most Common)

Patient alive/event-free up to time \(t\), true event time unknown but \(\geq t\).

Example: A patient enrolls at Month 0, is followed for 24 months, and is still alive at data cutoff. Their true survival time is \(\geq 24\) months.

Assumption: Censoring mechanism is independent of the outcome (non-informative censoring).

23.2.2 Left Censoring

Patient experienced the event before enrollment or observation began; we know the event happened before \(t\) but not exactly when.

Example: A patient reports they had a stroke sometime before the study started, but we don’t know the exact date.

23.2.3 Interval Censoring

Event occurred between two observation times.

Example: A patient was disease-free at Month 6 visit but had recurrence by Month 12. True recurrence time is between 6–12 months.

23.3 Core Survival Concepts

23.3.1 The Survival Function

The survival function \(S(t)\) is the probability that an individual survives beyond time \(t\):

\[S(t) = P(T > t) = 1 - F(t)\]

where \(T\) is the random variable representing time-to-event and \(F(t)\) is the cumulative distribution function.

Clinical interpretation: If \(S(12) = 0.75\) in a cancer trial, 75% of patients are expected to survive at least 12 months.

Properties: - \(S(0) = 1\) (everyone is alive at \(t=0\)) - \(S(\infty) = 0\) (eventually all patients experience the event) - Monotonically decreasing

23.3.2 The Hazard Function

The hazard function \(h(t)\) represents the instantaneous risk of the event at time \(t\), given survival up to time \(t\):

\[h(t) = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{P(t \leq T < t+\Delta t \mid T \geq t)}{\Delta t}\]

This is the conditional probability of event occurrence in the next small time interval, given the patient has survived to time \(t\).

Clinical interpretation: If \(h(t)\) increases over time (increasing hazard), risk worsens as patients age. If \(h(t)\) is constant (proportional hazards), risk does not depend on survival duration.

The hazard and survival functions are related:

\[h(t) = -\frac{d}{dt} \ln S(t)\]

and

\[S(t) = \exp\left(-\int_0^t h(u) \, du\right) = \exp\left(-H(t)\right)\]

where \(H(t) = \int_0^t h(u) \, du\) is the cumulative hazard.

23.4 Kaplan-Meier (KM) Estimator

In most clinical trials, we don’t know the true survival distribution. The Kaplan-Meier estimator is a non-parametric method to estimate \(S(t)\) from observed data.

23.4.1 Formula

\[\hat{S}(t) = \prod_{t_i \leq t} \left(1 - \frac{d_i}{n_i}\right)\]

where: - \(t_i\) are distinct event times - \(d_i\) = number of events at time \(t_i\) - \(n_i\) = number of patients at risk at time \(t_i\) (those still enrolled and event-free before \(t_i\))

23.4.2 Step-by-Step Calculation Example

Consider 10 patients followed for 12 months:

Time Events At Risk \(1 - \frac{d}{n}\) \(\hat{S}(t)\)
0 0 10 1.0 1.00
2 1 10 0.9 0.90
5 2 8 0.75 0.675
9 1 5 0.8 0.54
12 0 3 1.0 0.54

At \(t = 5\) months: \(\hat{S}(5) = 0.9 \times 0.75 = 0.675\)

23.4.3 Reading a Kaplan-Meier Curve

  • Vertical drops indicate event occurrences
  • Horizontal plateaus indicate no events
  • Confidence bands show uncertainty around the curve
  • Median survival: time at which \(\hat{S}(t) = 0.5\) (50% event rate)

23.4.4 Confidence Bands

Greenwood’s formula provides the variance:

\[\widehat{Var}[\ln \hat{S}(t)] = \sum_{t_i \leq t} \frac{d_i}{n_i(n_i - d_i)}\]

95% CI for \(S(t)\) is constructed on the log scale for stability.

23.5 Log-Rank Test

When comparing survival curves between two or more groups, the log-rank test is the standard hypothesis test.

23.5.1 Null Hypothesis

\[H_0: S_1(t) = S_2(t) \text{ for all } t\]

(Survival functions are identical between groups.)

23.5.2 Test Statistic

The log-rank test statistic follows a chi-squared distribution:

\[\chi^2 = \frac{(O_1 - E_1)^2}{E_1} + \frac{(O_2 - E_2)^2}{E_2}\]

where: - \(O_i\) = observed number of events in group \(i\) - \(E_i\) = expected number of events under the null hypothesis - Typically reported with 1 degree of freedom for two groups

Clinical interpretation: A \(p\)-value \(< 0.05\) suggests a significant difference in survival between groups.

23.5.3 Stratification

When comparing treatments while controlling for a prognostic factor (e.g., disease stage):

\[\chi^2_{stratified} = \frac{(O_1 - E_1)^2}{Var(O_1)}\]

summed over strata, removes confounding by the stratification variable.

23.6 Cox Proportional Hazards Model

The Cox proportional hazards (PH) model is the most widely used semi-parametric survival model in clinical research. It models how baseline hazard changes with covariates without assuming a specific distribution.

23.6.1 Model Formula

\[h(t \mid X) = h_0(t) \exp(\beta_1 X_1 + \beta_2 X_2 + \cdots + \beta_p X_p)\]

where: - \(h_0(t)\) is the baseline hazard (hazard when all covariates are zero) - \(X_j\) are covariates - \(\beta_j\) are log-hazard ratios

23.6.2 Hazard Ratio

For a one-unit increase in covariate \(X_j\):

\[HR = e^{\beta_j}\]

Clinical interpretation: - \(HR = 1\): no effect (reference) - \(HR = 0.65\): 35% reduction in hazard (more favorable) - \(HR = 1.5\): 50% increase in hazard (less favorable)

23.6.3 Example Interpretation

“Patients receiving treatment A had a 35% lower hazard of death compared to placebo (HR = 0.65, 95% CI: 0.48–0.88, \(p = 0.004\)).”

23.6.4 Proportional Hazards Assumption

A critical assumption: the hazard ratio between groups is constant over time. If violated, the Cox model may give misleading estimates.

Testing the PH assumption:

  1. Schoenfeld Residuals Test: Tests correlation between residuals and time.
    • \(p > 0.05\): assumption met
    • \(p < 0.05\): assumption violated
  2. Log-Log Survival Plot: Plot \(\ln[-\ln S(t)]\) vs \(\ln(t)\). Parallel lines indicate proportional hazards.

23.6.5 Stratified Cox Model

If PH assumption is violated for a variable, stratify by it:

\[h_i(t \mid X) = h_{0i}(t) \exp(\beta X)\]

Each stratum has its own baseline hazard but shares the same regression coefficients. The variable you stratify by is removed from the model.

23.7 Clinical Example: Lung Cancer Survival

In this example, we analyze the survival::lung dataset (North Central Cancer Treatment Group).

Study Context: Follow 228 patients with advanced lung cancer. Primary outcome is overall survival (OS). Covariates: age, sex, Eastern Cooperative Oncology Group (ECOG) performance status.

23.7.1 Exploratory Analysis and KM Curves

library(survival)
library(ggsurvfit)
library(tidyverse)

# Load lung cancer data
data(lung)

# Check data structure
head(lung)
str(lung)

# Prepare data
lung_clean <- lung %>%
  mutate(
    sex = factor(sex, levels = 1:2, labels = c("Male", "Female")),
    status = factor(status, levels = 1:2, labels = c("Censored", "Event"))
  )

# Fit Kaplan-Meier estimator (overall)
km_overall <- survfit(Surv(time, status) ~ 1, data = lung_clean)
summary(km_overall, times = c(30, 60, 180, 365))

# Kaplan-Meier by sex
km_sex <- survfit(Surv(time, status) ~ sex, data = lung_clean)
summary(km_sex)

# Beautiful KM plot using ggsurvfit
km_plot <- ggsurvfit(km_sex) +
  labs(
    title = "Overall Survival by Sex in Advanced Lung Cancer",
    subtitle = "Kaplan-Meier Curves (N=228)",
    x = "Time (days)",
    y = "Probability of Survival"
  ) +
  theme_minimal() +
  theme(
    plot.title = element_text(face = "bold", size = 14),
    legend.position = "inside",
    legend.position.inside = c(0.75, 0.25)
  )

print(km_plot)

Key Output: - Median OS for males: ~305 days - Median OS for females: ~392 days - This suggests females have longer survival, but statistical significance must be tested.

23.7.2 Log-Rank Test

# Log-rank test for sex
logrank_test <- survdiff(Surv(time, status) ~ sex, data = lung_clean)
print(logrank_test)

# Extract p-value and test statistic
logrank_p <- pchisq(logrank_test$chisq, df = 1, lower.tail = FALSE)
cat("Log-rank test p-value:", logrank_p, "\n")

Interpretation: If \(p < 0.05\), we reject the null hypothesis and conclude survival curves differ significantly between sexes.

23.7.3 Cox Proportional Hazards Model

# Fit Cox model with age, sex, and ECOG performance status
cox_model <- coxph(
  Surv(time, status) ~ age + sex + ph.ecog,
  data = lung_clean
)

# Model summary
summary(cox_model)

# Extract and display hazard ratios with CIs
hr_table <- tibble(
  Variable = names(cox_model$coefficients),
  Beta = cox_model$coefficients,
  HR = exp(cox_model$coefficients),
  SE = sqrt(diag(vcov(cox_model))),
) %>%
  mutate(
    CI_Lower = exp(Beta - 1.96 * SE),
    CI_Upper = exp(Beta + 1.96 * SE),
    `HR [95% CI]` = sprintf(
      "%.2f [%.2f–%.2f]",
      HR, CI_Lower, CI_Upper
    ),
    `p-value` = 1 - pchisq(
      (Beta / SE)^2, df = 1
    )
  ) %>%
  select(Variable, `HR [95% CI]`, `p-value`) %>%
  mutate(`p-value` = sprintf("%.4f", `p-value`))

print(hr_table)

Expected Output Interpretation: - Age: HR ~ 1.01 per year (each additional year increases hazard by 1%) - Sex (Female vs Male): HR ~ 0.58 (females have ~42% lower hazard of death) - ECOG: HR ~ 1.4–1.5 per unit (worse performance status increases hazard)

23.7.4 Testing the Proportional Hazards Assumption

# Test PH assumption using Schoenfeld residuals
ph_test <- cox.zph(cox_model)
print(ph_test)

# Plot log-log survival curves
plot(ph_test)

# If any variable has p < 0.05, investigate further
# Visualize by plotting cumulative hazard by group
library(ggplot2)

# Extract and plot cumulative hazard
km_by_sex <- survfit(Surv(time, status) ~ sex, data = lung_clean)

plot_data <- data.frame(
  time = rep(km_by_sex$time, 2),
  cumhaz = c(km_by_sex$cumhaz[1:length(km_by_sex$time[km_by_sex$strata == 2])],
             km_by_sex$cumhaz[(length(km_by_sex$time[km_by_sex$strata == 2]) + 1):
                              length(km_by_sex$cumhaz)]),
  sex = c(rep("Male", length(km_by_sex$time[km_by_sex$strata == 2])),
          rep("Female", sum(km_by_sex$strata == 2)))
)

# Create proper cumulative hazard plot using -log(S(t))
kmfit <- survfit(Surv(time, status) ~ sex, data = lung_clean)
surv_summary_data <- data.frame(
  time  = kmfit$time,
  surv  = kmfit$surv,
  strata = rep(names(kmfit$strata), times = kmfit$strata)
) %>%
  mutate(
    log_minus_log_S = log(-log(pmax(surv, 1e-10))),
    log_time = log(time)
  )

ph_plot <- ggplot(surv_summary_data, aes(x = log_time, y = log_minus_log_S,
                                         color = strata)) +
  geom_step() +
  labs(
    title = "Log-Log Survival Plot to Assess Proportional Hazards",
    x = "ln(Time)",
    y = "ln(-ln S(t))",
    color = "Group"
  ) +
  theme_minimal()

print(ph_plot)

Interpretation: - If lines are approximately parallel: proportional hazards assumption is met - If lines diverge: assumption violated; consider stratification or time-varying coefficients

23.7.5 Stratified Cox Model (if PH assumption violated)

# If ECOG violates PH assumption, stratify by it
cox_stratified <- coxph(
  Surv(time, status) ~ age + sex + strata(ph.ecog),
  data = lung_clean
)

summary(cox_stratified)

23.8 Key Clinical Takeaways

  1. Censoring is information: Never discard censored observations; they provide evidence the patient was event-free up to that time.

  2. KM curves are descriptive: Show survival patterns; log-rank test determines if differences are statistically significant.

  3. Median survival: Clinically meaningful; compare trial arms to assess treatment efficacy.

  4. Hazard ratios: Standard for reporting treatment effects in survival studies. Always include 95% CIs and \(p\)-values.

  5. Check assumptions: Proportional hazards assumption is critical; test it and adjust (stratify) if violated.

  6. Context matters: Interpret HR in relation to clinical significance, not just statistical significance.

### The Survival Function

$$S(t) = P(T > t) = 1 - F(t)$$

where $T$ is the random variable representing time-to-event and $F(t)$ is the cumulative distribution function.

**Clinical interpretation**: If $S(12) = 0.75$ in a cancer trial, 75% of patients are expected to survive at least 12 months.

Properties:
- $S(0) = 1$ (everyone is alive at $t=0$)
- $S(\infty) = 0$ (eventually all patients experience the event)
- Monotonically decreasing

### The Hazard Function

$$h(t) = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{P(t \leq T < t+\Delta t \mid T \geq t)}{\Delta t}$$

$$h(t) = -\frac{d}{dt} \ln S(t)$$

and

$$S(t) = \exp\left(-\int_0^t h(u) \, du\right) = \exp\left(-H(t)\right)$$

where $H(t) = \int_0^t h(u) \, du$ is the **cumulative hazard**.

## Kaplan-Meier (KM) Estimator

In most clinical trials, we don't know the true survival distribution. The **Kaplan-Meier estimator** is a non-parametric method to estimate $S(t)$ from observed data.

### Formula

$$\hat{S}(t) = \prod_{t_i \leq t} \left(1 - \frac{d_i}{n_i}\right)$$

where:
- $t_i$ are distinct event times
- $d_i$ = number of events at time $t_i$
- $n_i$ = number of patients at risk at time $t_i$ (those still enrolled and event-free before $t_i$)

### Step-by-Step Calculation Example

Consider 10 patients followed for 12 months:

| Time | Events | At Risk | $1 - \frac{d}{n}$ | $\hat{S}(t)$ |
|------|--------|---------|------------------|------------|
| 0    | 0      | 10      | 1.0              | 1.00       |
| 2    | 1      | 10      | 0.9              | 0.90       |
| 5    | 2      | 8       | 0.75             | 0.675      |
| 9    | 1      | 5       | 0.8              | 0.54       |
| 12   | 0      | 3       | 1.0              | 0.54       |

At $t = 5$ months: $\hat{S}(5) = 0.9 \times 0.75 = 0.675$

### Reading a Kaplan-Meier Curve

- **Vertical drops** indicate event occurrences
- **Horizontal plateaus** indicate no events
- **Confidence bands** show uncertainty around the curve
- **Median survival**: time at which $\hat{S}(t) = 0.5$ (50% event rate)

### Confidence Bands

Greenwood's formula provides the variance:

$$\widehat{Var}[\ln \hat{S}(t)] = \sum_{t_i \leq t} \frac{d_i}{n_i(n_i - d_i)}$$

95% CI for $S(t)$ is constructed on the log scale for stability.

## Log-Rank Test

When comparing survival curves between two or more groups, the **log-rank test** is the standard hypothesis test.

### Null Hypothesis

$$H_0: S_1(t) = S_2(t) \text{ for all } t$$

(Survival functions are identical between groups.)

### Test Statistic

The log-rank test statistic follows a chi-squared distribution:

$$\chi^2 = \frac{(O_1 - E_1)^2}{E_1} + \frac{(O_2 - E_2)^2}{E_2}$$

where:
- $O_i$ = observed number of events in group $i$
- $E_i$ = expected number of events under the null hypothesis
- Typically reported with 1 degree of freedom for two groups

**Clinical interpretation**: A $p$-value $< 0.05$ suggests a significant difference in survival between groups.

### Stratification

When comparing treatments while controlling for a prognostic factor (e.g., disease stage):

$$\chi^2_{stratified} = \frac{(O_1 - E_1)^2}{Var(O_1)}$$

summed over strata, removes confounding by the stratification variable.

## Cox Proportional Hazards Model

The **Cox proportional hazards (PH) model** is the most widely used semi-parametric survival model in clinical research. It models how baseline hazard changes with covariates without assuming a specific distribution.

### Model Formula

$$h(t \mid X) = h_0(t) \exp(\beta_1 X_1 + \beta_2 X_2 + \cdots + \beta_p X_p)$$

where:
- $h_0(t)$ is the **baseline hazard** (hazard when all covariates are zero)
- $X_j$ are covariates
- $\beta_j$ are log-hazard ratios

### Hazard Ratio

For a one-unit increase in covariate $X_j$:

$$HR = e^{\beta_j}$$

**Clinical interpretation**:
- $HR = 1$: no effect (reference)
- $HR = 0.65$: 35% **reduction** in hazard (more favorable)
- $HR = 1.5$: 50% **increase** in hazard (less favorable)

### Example Interpretation

"Patients receiving treatment A had a 35% lower hazard of death compared to placebo (HR = 0.65, 95% CI: 0.48–0.88, $p = 0.004$)."

### Proportional Hazards Assumption

A critical assumption: the hazard ratio between groups is constant over time. If violated, the Cox model may give misleading estimates.

**Testing the PH assumption**:

1. **Schoenfeld Residuals Test**: Tests correlation between residuals and time.
   - $p > 0.05$: assumption met
   - $p < 0.05$: assumption violated

2. **Log-Log Survival Plot**: Plot $\ln[-\ln S(t)]$ vs $\ln(t)$. Parallel lines indicate proportional hazards.

### Stratified Cox Model

If PH assumption is violated for a variable, stratify by it:

$$h_i(t \mid X) = h_{0i}(t) \exp(\beta X)$$

Each stratum has its own baseline hazard but shares the same regression coefficients. The variable you stratify by is removed from the model.

## Clinical Example: Lung Cancer Survival

In this example, we analyze the `survival::lung` dataset (North Central Cancer Treatment Group).

**Study Context**: Follow 228 patients with advanced lung cancer. Primary outcome is overall survival (OS). Covariates: age, sex, Eastern Cooperative Oncology Group (ECOG) performance status.

### Exploratory Analysis and KM Curves

```r
library(survival)
library(ggsurvfit)
library(tidyverse)

# Load lung cancer data
data(lung)

lung_clean <- lung %>%
```

**Key Output**:
- Median OS for males: ~305 days
- Median OS for females: ~392 days

### Log-Rank Test

```r
# Log-rank test for sex
logrank_test <- survdiff(Surv(time, status) ~ sex, data = lung_clean)
print(logrank_test)

# Extract p-value and test statistic
logrank_p <- pchisq(logrank_test$chisq, df = 1, lower.tail = FALSE)
cat("Log-rank test p-value:", logrank_p, "\n")
```

**Interpretation**: If $p < 0.05$, we reject the null hypothesis and conclude survival curves differ significantly between sexes.

### Cox Proportional Hazards Model

```r
# Fit Cox model with age, sex, and ECOG performance status
cox_model <- coxph(
  Surv(time, status) ~ age + sex + ph.ecog,
  data = lung_clean
)

# Model summary
summary(cox_model)

# Extract and display hazard ratios with CIs
hr_table <- tibble(
  Variable = names(cox_model$coefficients),
  Beta = cox_model$coefficients,
  HR = exp(cox_model$coefficients),
  SE = sqrt(diag(vcov(cox_model))),
) %>%
  mutate(
    CI_Lower = exp(Beta - 1.96 * SE),
    CI_Upper = exp(Beta + 1.96 * SE),
    `HR [95% CI]` = sprintf(
      "%.2f [%.2f–%.2f]",
      HR, CI_Lower, CI_Upper
    ),
    `p-value` = 1 - pchisq(
      (Beta / SE)^2, df = 1
    )
  ) %>%
  select(Variable, `HR [95% CI]`, `p-value`) %>%
  mutate(`p-value` = sprintf("%.4f", `p-value`))

print(hr_table)
```

**Expected Output Interpretation**:
- **Age**: HR ~ 1.01 per year (each additional year increases hazard by 1%)
- **Sex (Female vs Male)**: HR ~ 0.58 (females have ~42% lower hazard of death)
- **ECOG**: HR ~ 1.4–1.5 per unit (worse performance status increases hazard)

### Testing the Proportional Hazards Assumption

```r
# Test PH assumption using Schoenfeld residuals
ph_test <- cox.zph(cox_model)
print(ph_test)

# Plot log-log survival curves
plot(ph_test)

# If any variable has p < 0.05, investigate further
# Visualize by plotting cumulative hazard by group
library(ggplot2)

# Extract and plot cumulative hazard
km_by_sex <- survfit(Surv(time, status) ~ sex, data = lung_clean)

plot_data <- data.frame(
  time = rep(km_by_sex$time, 2),
  cumhaz = c(km_by_sex$cumhaz[1:length(km_by_sex$time[km_by_sex$strata == 2])],
             km_by_sex$cumhaz[(length(km_by_sex$time[km_by_sex$strata == 2]) + 1):
                              length(km_by_sex$cumhaz)]),
  sex = c(rep("Male", length(km_by_sex$time[km_by_sex$strata == 2])),
          rep("Female", sum(km_by_sex$strata == 2)))
)

# Create proper cumulative hazard plot using -log(S(t))
kmfit <- survfit(Surv(time, status) ~ sex, data = lung_clean)
surv_summary_data <- data.frame(
  time  = kmfit$time,
  surv  = kmfit$surv,
  strata = rep(names(kmfit$strata), times = kmfit$strata)
) %>%
  mutate(
    log_minus_log_S = log(-log(pmax(surv, 1e-10))),
    log_time = log(time)
  )

ph_plot <- ggplot(surv_summary_data, aes(x = log_time, y = log_minus_log_S,
                                         color = strata)) +
  geom_step() +
  labs(
    title = "Log-Log Survival Plot to Assess Proportional Hazards",
    x = "ln(Time)",
    y = "ln(-ln S(t))",
    color = "Group"
  ) +
  theme_minimal()

print(ph_plot)
```

**Interpretation**:
- If lines are approximately **parallel**: proportional hazards assumption is met
- If lines **diverge**: assumption violated; consider stratification or time-varying coefficients

## Key Clinical Takeaways

1. **Censoring is information**: Never discard censored observations; they provide evidence the patient was event-free up to that time.

2. **KM curves are descriptive**: Show survival patterns; log-rank test determines if differences are statistically significant.

3. **Median survival**: Clinically meaningful; compare trial arms to assess treatment efficacy.

4. **Hazard ratios**: Standard for reporting treatment effects in survival studies. Always include 95% CIs and $p$-values.

5. **Check assumptions**: Proportional hazards assumption is critical; test it and adjust (stratify) if violated.

6. **Context matters**: Interpret HR in relation to clinical significance, not just statistical significance.
