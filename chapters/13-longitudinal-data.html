24  Longitudinal Data Analysis

24.1 What Makes Longitudinal Data Different?

Longitudinal data are repeated measurements on the same subjects over time. This structure is fundamental in clinical trials and observational studies but violates the independence assumption of classical statistics.

24.1.1 The Core Challenge: Within-Subject Correlation

In traditional linear regression, observations are assumed independent: \(\text{Cov}(Y_i, Y_j) = 0\) for \(i \neq j\). In longitudinal data, multiple measurements from the same patient are correlated:

\[\text{Cov}(Y_{it}, Y_{is}) \neq 0 \text{ for } t \neq s\]

This correlation arises because: - Biology: Patients with naturally high baseline values tend to remain high - Study design: Same person measured repeatedly (inherent dependence) - Patient effects: Individual characteristics (metabolism, age, genetics) affect all their measurements

Consequence: Ignoring within-subject correlation: - Biases standard errors (usually underestimated) - Inflates test statistics and \(p\)-values - Yields overly narrow confidence intervals - Can lead to false positive findings

24.1.2 Example: Clinical Trial with Repeated Measures

A 12-week antihypertensive trial measures systolic blood pressure (SBP) at baseline, weeks 4, 8, and 12 in \(n=100\) patients. We have \(100 \times 4 = 400\) observations, but they represent only 100 independent units (patients). Treating all 400 as independent would artificially inflate power.

24.2 Why Not Just Use Repeated Measures ANOVA?

Repeated Measures ANOVA (RM-ANOVA) was the historical standard, but has critical limitations:

Aspect RM-ANOVA Mixed Models
Missing data Requires complete cases; loses power Handles missing data (MAR assumption)
Unequal spacing Assumes equal intervals Flexible; works with any timing
Continuous covariates Limited Full regression framework
Unbalanced designs Problematic Naturally accommodates
Correlation structures Compound symmetry only Exchangeable, AR(1), unstructured

Conclusion: Mixed models are superior for modern clinical data.

24.3 Linear Mixed-Effects Models

24.3.1 Model Specification

The linear mixed-effects (LME) model partitions variability into fixed effects (population-level) and random effects (subject-specific):

\[Y_{ij} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 t_{ij} + b_{0i} + b_{1i} t_{ij} + \epsilon_{ij}\]

where: - \(Y_{ij}\) = response for subject \(i\) at time \(j\) - \(\beta_0\) = fixed intercept (population average at \(t=0\)) - \(\beta_1\) = fixed slope (population average change per time unit) - \(b_{0i}\) = random intercept for subject \(i\) (deviation from population intercept) - \(b_{1i}\) = random slope for subject \(i\) (deviation from population slope) - \(\epsilon_{ij}\) = within-subject residual error at time \(j\) for subject \(i\)

24.3.2 Assumptions

Random effects: \[\begin{pmatrix} b_{0i} \\ b_{1i} \end{pmatrix} \sim N\left(\begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}, \begin{pmatrix} \sigma_b^2 & \rho \sigma_{b0} \sigma_{b1} \\ \rho \sigma_{b0} \sigma_{b1} & \sigma_{b1}^2 \end{pmatrix}\right)\]

Within-subject errors: \[\epsilon_{ij} \sim N(0, \sigma_e^2)\]

Independence: Random effects and residual errors are independent.

24.3.3 Interpretation of Effects

  • Fixed effects (\(\beta\)): Population-level average trends; what we report in papers
  • Random intercepts (\(b_{0i}\)): Individual baseline differences; allows “each patient has their own intercept”
  • Random slopes (\(b_{1i}\)): Individual response trajectories; allows “treatment effect differs per patient”

24.3.4 Correlation Structures

The correlation structure specifies how repeated measures within a subject relate over time.

Compound Symmetry (Exchangeable)

Correlation is constant across all time pairs: \(\text{Corr}(Y_{it}, Y_{is}) = \rho\) for all \(t \neq s\).

\[\text{Corr matrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & \rho & \rho \\ \rho & 1 & \rho \\ \rho & \rho & 1 \end{pmatrix}\]

Assumption: Observations equally correlated regardless of time gap. Appropriate when: - Visit schedule is irregular - No temporal trend in correlation

Not appropriate for: - Long-term follow-up where recent measurements are more similar - Data with natural decay in correlation over time

Autoregressive AR(1)

Correlation decays with time distance: \(\text{Corr}(Y_{it}, Y_{is}) = \rho^{|t-s|}\).

\[\text{Corr matrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & \rho & \rho^2 \\ \rho & 1 & \rho \\ \rho^2 & \rho & 1 \end{pmatrix}\]

Assumption: Measurements closer in time are more similar. Appropriate when: - Regular visit schedule (e.g., monthly, weekly) - Biological/natural decay in correlation - Long-term follow-up studies

Example: In a weekly symptom diary, Week 1 vs Week 2 more correlated than Week 1 vs Week 12.

Unstructured

Allows unique correlation for each time pair. Maximum flexibility, but requires more parameters.

\[\text{Corr matrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & \rho_{12} & \rho_{13} \\ \rho_{12} & 1 & \rho_{23} \\ \rho_{13} & \rho_{23} & 1 \end{pmatrix}\]

Use when: Sample size is large and structured patterns are implausible.

24.3.5 Estimation: REML vs ML

  • REML (Restricted Maximum Likelihood): Accounts for uncertainty in fixed effects; preferred for parameter estimation. Better for inference on variance components.

  • ML (Maximum Likelihood): Overestimates variance; preferred for model comparison (likelihood ratio tests). Biased variances but valid test statistics.

Practical rule: Use REML for final inference; use ML for model selection (AIC comparison).

24.3.6 Model Comparison Using AIC

Akaike Information Criterion balances model fit with complexity:

\[\text{AIC} = -2 \log(L) + 2k\]

where \(L\) is the likelihood and \(k\) is the number of parameters.

Interpretation: - Lower AIC is better - \(\Delta \text{AIC} > 10\): strong evidence favoring lower model - \(\Delta \text{AIC} < 3\): models are comparable; prefer simpler

Example: AIC comparison to decide between random intercept only vs random intercept + slope.

24.4 Generalized Linear Mixed Models (GLMM)

For non-normal outcomes (binary, count):

\[\eta_{ij} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 t_{ij} + b_{0i}\]

\[g(E[Y_{ij}]) = \eta_{ij}\]

where \(g\) is a link function (logit for binary, log for count).

Example: Model probability of adverse event occurrence over trial visits using logistic GLMM.

24.5 Handling Dropouts: Missing At Random (MAR)

Mixed models rely on the Missing At Random (MAR) assumption:

\[P(R = 1 \mid Y_{obs}, Y_{mis}) = P(R = 1 \mid Y_{obs})\]

Missingness depends only on observed data, not on unobserved values.

Example: A patient drops out because their baseline depression (observed) was severe—this is MAR. However, if they drop out because their depression improved dramatically (unobserved)—this is MNAR.

Practical implication: Include baseline and prognostic variables in the model to approach MAR; sensitivity analyses for MNAR scenarios.

24.6 Mixed Model for Repeated Measures (MMRM)

The MMRM is the regulatory standard (ICH E9(R1)) for clinical trials:

\[Y_{ij} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 V_j + \beta_2 T_i + \beta_3 (V_j \times T_i) + b_{0i} + \epsilon_{ij}\]

where: - \(V_j\) = visit indicator - \(T_i\) = treatment group - \(V_j \times T_i\) = treatment-by-visit interaction (captures differential change) - \(b_{0i}\) = random intercept only (no random slopes) - \(\epsilon_{ij}\) = visit-level residual with unstructured correlation

Key features: - Includes baseline value as a covariate (not as outcome) - Visit as a factor (not linear time) for flexibility - Unstructured correlation (most flexible) - Robust to missing data (MAR)

Clinical interpretation: Report predicted mean values at each visit by treatment arm with CIs, and treatment difference at final visit.

24.7 Clinical Example 1: Orthodontic Growth

Using the nlme::Orthodont dataset: mandibular distance (bone growth) in 27 children, measured at ages 8, 10, 12, 14 years.

24.7.1 Exploratory Analysis

library(nlme)
library(lme4)
library(tidyverse)
library(ggplot2)

data(Orthodont)

# Data structure
head(Orthodont)
# Subject: child ID
# age: measurement age
# distance: mandibular distance (mm)

# Visualize individual trajectories
plot_indiv <- ggplot(Orthodont, aes(x = age, y = distance,
                                     group = Subject, color = Subject)) +
  geom_line(alpha = 0.6) +
  geom_point(size = 2) +
  facet_wrap(~Subject) +
  labs(
    title = "Individual Orthodontic Growth Trajectories",
    x = "Age (years)",
    y = "Mandibular Distance (mm)",
    subtitle = "27 children measured at 4 time points"
  ) +
  theme_minimal() +
  theme(legend.position = "none")

print(plot_indiv)

# Plot individual trajectories with population mean overlay
plot_pop <- ggplot(Orthodont, aes(x = age, y = distance)) +
  geom_line(aes(group = Subject), alpha = 0.3, color = "gray") +
  stat_summary(fun = mean, geom = "line", size = 1.5,
               color = "red", aes(group = 1)) +
  stat_summary(fun = mean, geom = "point", size = 3,
               color = "red") +
  labs(
    title = "Orthodontic Growth: Individual + Population Mean",
    x = "Age (years)",
    y = "Mandibular Distance (mm)"
  ) +
  theme_minimal()

print(plot_pop)

24.7.2 Fitting Linear Mixed Models

# Model 1: Random intercept only (each child different baseline)
lme1 <- lme(distance ~ age, data = Orthodont,
            random = ~ 1 | Subject,
            method = "REML")

summary(lme1)

# Model 2: Random intercept + slope (each child different trajectory)
lme2 <- lme(distance ~ age, data = Orthodont,
            random = ~ age | Subject,
            method = "REML")

summary(lme2)

# Model 3: Using lme4 with explicit correlation structure
library(lme4)
lme3 <- lmer(distance ~ age + (1 | Subject), data = Orthodont, REML = TRUE)
summary(lme3)

# Extract fixed effects
fixef(lme3)
# Intercept: ~16 mm at age 0 (baseline mandibular distance)
# Age slope: ~0.66 mm/year (average growth rate)

# Extract random effects
ranef(lme3)
# Shows deviation of each child's intercept from population mean

24.7.3 Model Comparison

# Compare models using likelihood ratio test
# First refit with ML for testing
lme1_ml <- update(lme1, method = "ML")
lme2_ml <- update(lme2, method = "ML")

anova(lme1_ml, lme2_ml)

# Extract AIC
AIC(lme1, lme2)
# If ΔAIC > 10, prefer the model with lower AIC

Interpretation Example: - Fixed effects: Population-level growth rate is 0.66 mm/year - Random intercept variance: \(\sigma_{b0}^2 = 1.8\) suggests substantial baseline differences (~4.2 mm range between children) - Random slope variance: \(\sigma_{b1}^2 = 0.08\) suggests growth rates are similar across children

24.7.4 Predicted Trajectories

# Create new data for prediction
new_ages <- expand.grid(

24.8 Clinical Example 2: MMRM for Vital Signs

Using pharmaversesdtm::vs dataset: systolic blood pressure (SBP) measured at baseline, Week 2, Week 4, Week 8, Week 12 in a hypertension trial.

library(pharmaversesdtm)
library(admiral)
library(gtsummary)

# Load and prepare data
data(vs)
data(dm)

# Filter SBP measurements and prepare for analysis
vs_sbp <- vs %>%
  filter(VSTESTCD == "SYSBP") %>%  # Systolic BP
  left_join(dm %>% select(USUBJID, ARM), by = "USUBJID") %>%
  mutate(
    VISIT = factor(VISIT,
                   levels = c("Baseline", "Week 2", "Week 4", "Week 8", "Week 12")),
    VISITN = as.numeric(VISIT),
    TRTA = factor(ARM),  # Treatment arm from demographics
  ) %>%
  select(USUBJID, VISIT, VISITN, VSSTRESN, TRTA) %>%
  rename(SBP = VSSTRESN) %>%
  arrange(USUBJID, VISITN)

# Get baseline SBP for each subject
baseline_sbp <- vs_sbp %>%
  filter(VISITN == 1) %>%
  select(USUBJID, SBP) %>%
  rename(baseline_SBP = SBP) %>%
  distinct()

vs_sbp <- vs_sbp %>%
  left_join(baseline_sbp, by = "USUBJID")

# Exploratory plot: treatment mean trajectories
plot_means <- vs_sbp %>%
  group_by(VISIT, TRTA) %>%
  summarise(
    mean_sbp = mean(SBP, na.rm = TRUE),
    se_sbp = sd(SBP, na.rm = TRUE) / sqrt(n()),
    .groups = "drop"
  ) %>%
  ggplot(aes(x = VISIT, y = mean_sbp, color = TRTA, group = TRTA)) +
  geom_line(size = 1) +
  geom_point(size = 3) +
  geom_errorbar(aes(ymin = mean_sbp - se_sbp, ymax = mean_sbp + se_sbp),
                width = 0.2) +
  labs(
    title = "SBP Response by Treatment Arm",
    x = "Visit",
    y = "Systolic Blood Pressure (mmHg)",
    color = "Treatment"
  ) +
  theme_minimal() +
  theme(axis.text.x = element_text(angle = 45, hjust = 1))

print(plot_means)

24.8.1 MMRM Analysis

# MMRM using lme4 (or nlme with unstructured correlation via gls)
library(lme4)

# Fit MMRM
mmrm_model <- lmer(
  SBP ~ VISIT * TRTA + (1 | USUBJID),
  data = vs_sbp,
  REML = TRUE,
  na.action = na.omit
)

summary(mmrm_model)

# Extract fixed effects (treatment comparisons)
fixed_effects <- fixef(mmrm_model)

# Interpretation example:
# TRTADrug A:VISITWeek 12: -8.5 mmHg
# Interpretation: Patients on Drug A had 8.5 mmHg greater reduction from baseline
# by Week 12 compared to Placebo

# Predicted means by treatment and visit
library(emmeans)

pred_means <- emmeans(mmrm_model, ~ VISIT | TRTA)
summary(pred_means)

# Contrast: Drug A vs Placebo at Week 12
contrast_w12 <- emmeans(mmrm_model, pairwise ~ TRTA | VISIT)
summary(contrast_w12)

24.8.2 Publication-Ready Output

# Create a summary table: mean ± SD by visit and treatment
vs_summary <- vs_sbp %>%
  group_by(VISIT, TRTA) %>%
  summarise(
    N = n(),
    Mean = mean(SBP, na.rm = TRUE),
    SD = sd(SBP, na.rm = TRUE),
    .groups = "drop"
  ) %>%
  mutate(
    `Mean (SD)` = sprintf("%.1f (%.1f)", Mean, SD)
  ) %>%
  select(VISIT, TRTA, N, `Mean (SD)`) %>%
  pivot_wider(
    names_from = TRTA,
    values_from = `Mean (SD)`,
    id_cols = VISIT
  )

print(vs_summary)

# Estimated treatment difference at final visit using tidy method
if (requireNamespace("broom.mixed", quietly = TRUE)) {
  library(broom.mixed)

  model_tidy <- tidy(mmrm_model, effects = "fixed")

  # lme4 does not compute p-values; approximate via z-statistic
  model_tidy <- model_tidy %>%
    mutate(p.value = 2 * (1 - pnorm(abs(statistic))))

  model_tidy %>%
    filter(grepl("Week 12", term)) %>%
    mutate(
      HR = exp(estimate),
      CI_L = exp(estimate - 1.96 * std.error),
      CI_U = exp(estimate + 1.96 * std.error)
    ) %>%
    select(intersect(names(model_tidy), c("term", "estimate", "std.error", "statistic", "p.value")))
}

The **correlation structure** specifies how repeated measures within a subject relate over time.

#### Compound Symmetry (Exchangeable)

Correlation is constant across all time pairs: $\text{Corr}(Y_{it}, Y_{is}) = \rho$ for all $t \neq s$.

$$\text{Corr matrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & \rho & \rho \\ \rho & 1 & \rho \\ \rho & \rho & 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

**Assumption**: Observations equally correlated regardless of time gap. Appropriate when:
- Visit schedule is irregular
- No temporal trend in correlation

**Not appropriate** for:
- Long-term follow-up where recent measurements are more similar
- Data with natural decay in correlation over time

#### Autoregressive AR(1)

Correlation decays with time distance: $\text{Corr}(Y_{it}, Y_{is}) = \rho^{|t-s|}$.

$$\text{Corr matrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & \rho & \rho^2 \\ \rho & 1 & \rho \\ \rho^2 & \rho & 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

**Assumption**: Measurements closer in time are more similar. Appropriate when:
- Regular visit schedule (e.g., monthly, weekly)
- Biological/natural decay in correlation
- Long-term follow-up studies

**Example**: In a weekly symptom diary, Week 1 vs Week 2 more correlated than Week 1 vs Week 12.

#### Unstructured

Allows unique correlation for each time pair. Maximum flexibility, but requires more parameters.

$$\text{Corr matrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & \rho_{12} & \rho_{13} \\ \rho_{12} & 1 & \rho_{23} \\ \rho_{13} & \rho_{23} & 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

**Use when**: Sample size is large and structured patterns are implausible.

### Estimation: REML vs ML

- **REML (Restricted Maximum Likelihood)**: Accounts for uncertainty in fixed effects; preferred for **parameter estimation**. Better for inference on variance components.

- **ML (Maximum Likelihood)**: Overestimates variance; preferred for **model comparison** (likelihood ratio tests). Biased variances but valid test statistics.

**Practical rule**: Use REML for final inference; use ML for model selection (AIC comparison).

### Model Comparison Using AIC

Akaike Information Criterion balances model fit with complexity:

$$\text{AIC} = -2 \log(L) + 2k$$

where $L$ is the likelihood and $k$ is the number of parameters.

**Interpretation**:
- Lower AIC is better
- $\Delta \text{AIC} > 10$: strong evidence favoring lower model
- $\Delta \text{AIC} < 3$: models are comparable; prefer simpler

**Example**: AIC comparison to decide between random intercept only vs random intercept + slope.

## Generalized Linear Mixed Models (GLMM)

For **non-normal outcomes** (binary, count):

$$\eta_{ij} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 t_{ij} + b_{0i}$$

$$g(E[Y_{ij}]) = \eta_{ij}$$

where $g$ is a link function (logit for binary, log for count).

Example: Model probability of adverse event occurrence over trial visits using logistic GLMM.

## Handling Dropouts: Missing At Random (MAR)

Mixed models rely on the **Missing At Random (MAR)** assumption:

$$P(R = 1 \mid Y_{obs}, Y_{mis}) = P(R = 1 \mid Y_{obs})$$

Missingness depends only on observed data, not on unobserved values.

**Example**: A patient drops out because their baseline depression (observed) was severe—this is MAR. However, if they drop out because their depression improved dramatically (unobserved)—this is MNAR.

**Practical implication**: Include baseline and prognostic variables in the model to approach MAR; sensitivity analyses for MNAR scenarios.

## Mixed Model for Repeated Measures (MMRM)

The **MMRM** is the regulatory standard (ICH E9(R1)) for clinical trials:

$$Y_{ij} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 V_j + \beta_2 T_i + \beta_3 (V_j \times T_i) + b_{0i} + \epsilon_{ij}$$

where:
- $V_j$ = visit indicator
- $T_i$ = treatment group
- $V_j \times T_i$ = treatment-by-visit interaction (captures differential change)
- $b_{0i}$ = random intercept only (no random slopes)
- $\epsilon_{ij}$ = visit-level residual with unstructured correlation

**Key features**:
- Includes **baseline value as a covariate** (not as outcome)
- **Visit as a factor** (not linear time) for flexibility
- **Unstructured correlation** (most flexible)
- Robust to missing data (MAR)

**Clinical interpretation**: Report predicted mean values at each visit by treatment arm with CIs, and treatment difference at final visit.

### Fitting Linear Mixed Models

```r
# Model 1: Random intercept only (each child different baseline)
lme1 <- lme(distance ~ age, data = Orthodont,
            random = ~ 1 | Subject,
            method = "REML")

summary(lme1)

# Model 2: Random intercept + slope (each child different trajectory)
lme2 <- lme(distance ~ age, data = Orthodont,
            random = ~ age | Subject,
            method = "REML")

summary(lme2)

# Model 3: Using lme4 with explicit correlation structure
library(lme4)
lme3 <- lmer(distance ~ age + (1 | Subject), data = Orthodont, REML = TRUE)
summary(lme3)

# Extract fixed effects
fixef(lme3)
# Intercept: ~16 mm at age 0 (baseline mandibular distance)
# Age slope: ~0.66 mm/year (average growth rate)

# Extract random effects
ranef(lme3)
# Shows deviation of each child's intercept from population mean
```

### Model Comparison

```r
# Compare models using likelihood ratio test
# First refit with ML for testing
lme1_ml <- update(lme1, method = "ML")
lme2_ml <- update(lme2, method = "ML")

anova(lme1_ml, lme2_ml)

# Extract AIC
AIC(lme1, lme2)
# If ΔAIC > 10, prefer the model with lower AIC
```

**Interpretation Example**:
- **Fixed effects**: Population-level growth rate is 0.66 mm/year
- **Random intercept variance**: $\sigma_{b0}^2 = 1.8$ suggests substantial baseline differences (~4.2 mm range between children)
- **Random slope variance**: $\sigma_{b1}^2 = 0.08$ suggests growth rates are similar across children

## Clinical Example 2: MMRM for Vital Signs

Using `pharmaversesdtm::vs` dataset: systolic blood pressure (SBP) measured at baseline, Week 2, Week 4, Week 8, Week 12 in a hypertension trial.

```r
library(pharmaversesdtm)
library(admiral)
library(gtsummary)

# Load and prepare data
data(vs)
data(dm)

# Filter SBP measurements and prepare for analysis
vs_sbp <- vs %>%
  filter(VSTESTCD == "SYSBP") %>%  # Systolic BP
  left_join(dm %>% select(USUBJID, ARM), by = "USUBJID") %>%
  mutate(
    VISIT = factor(VISIT,
                   levels = c("Baseline", "Week 2", "Week 4", "Week 8", "Week 12")),
    VISITN = as.numeric(VISIT),
    TRTA = factor(ARM),  # Treatment arm from demographics
  ) %>%
  select(USUBJID, VISIT, VISITN, VSSTRESN, TRTA) %>%
  rename(SBP = VSSTRESN) %>%
  arrange(USUBJID, VISITN)

# Get baseline SBP for each subject
baseline_sbp <- vs_sbp %>%
  filter(VISITN == 1) %>%
  select(USUBJID, SBP) %>%
  rename(baseline_SBP = SBP) %>%
  distinct()

vs_sbp <- vs_sbp %>%
  left_join(baseline_sbp, by = "USUBJID")

# Exploratory plot: treatment mean trajectories
plot_means <- vs_sbp %>%
  group_by(VISIT, TRTA) %>%
  summarise(
    mean_sbp = mean(SBP, na.rm = TRUE),
    se_sbp = sd(SBP, na.rm = TRUE) / sqrt(n()),
    .groups = "drop"
  ) %>%
  ggplot(aes(x = VISIT, y = mean_sbp, color = TRTA, group = TRTA)) +
  geom_line(size = 1) +
  geom_point(size = 3) +
  geom_errorbar(aes(ymin = mean_sbp - se_sbp, ymax = mean_sbp + se_sbp),
                width = 0.2) +

    x = "Visit",
    y = "Systolic Blood Pressure (mmHg)",
    color = "Treatment"

  theme_minimal() +
```

```r
  model_tidy <- model_tidy %>%

    mutate(

      CI_L = exp(estimate - 1.96 * std.error),
      CI_U = exp(estimate + 1.96 * std.error)
```

## Clinical Interpretation Summary

| Component | What It Means | How to Report |
|-----------|---------------|---------------|
| Fixed intercept | Population average at baseline | "Mean SBP at baseline was 145 mmHg" |
| Fixed slope | Population average change per unit time | "SBP decreased by 8 mmHg per month on average" |
| Treatment effect (fixed) | Differential treatment response | "Drug A reduced SBP by 12 mmHg more than placebo (95% CI: -18 to -6)" |
| Random intercept variance | Individual baseline variation | "Individual baselines ranged ~±10 mmHg from population mean" |
| Random slope variance | Individual response variation | "Response rates varied substantially across patients" |
