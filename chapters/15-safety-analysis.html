26  Safety Analysis in Clinical Trials

26.1 Introduction: Why Safety Analysis Matters

While efficacy demonstrates that a treatment works, safety analysis documents how well patients tolerate it. Regulatory agencies (FDA, EMA) require comprehensive safety assessments before drug approval. Safety findings can limit patient populations, require monitoring strategies, or prevent approval entirely.

26.1.1 Key Safety Endpoints

  • Adverse Events (AEs): Any undesired medical occurrence (rash, nausea, elevated liver enzymes)
  • Serious Adverse Events (SAEs): Life-threatening, result in hospitalization, disability, or death
  • Adverse Events of Special Interest (AESIs): Clinically important events predetermined as requiring careful monitoring (e.g., myocardial infarction in cardiovascular trials)
  • Dose Limiting Toxicity (DLT): Event requiring dose reduction or discontinuation
  • Discontinuation: Patient stops treatment due to adverse event

26.2 MedDRA Coding and Hierarchy

Medical Dictionary for Regulatory Activities (MedDRA) is the standard for coding clinical safety terms globally.

26.2.1 MedDRA Structure

The hierarchy from general to specific:

  1. System Organ Class (SOC): Highest level (e.g., “Nervous System Disorders”, “Gastrointestinal Disorders”)
  2. High Level Group Term (HLGT): Groups related preferred terms
  3. High Level Term (HLT): Intermediate grouping
  4. Preferred Term (PT): Standardized term used in reporting (e.g., “Headache”, “Nausea”)
  5. Lowest Level Term (LLT): Actual patient-reported symptom (e.g., “Bad headache”, “Feeling sick”)

26.2.2 Example Hierarchy

SOC: Nervous System Disorders
  HLGT: Headaches
    HLT: Headaches (excl migraine)
      PT: Headache
        LLT: Bad headache, Severe headache, Headache worse

Clinical Practice: - Data collection typically uses LLTs (patient language) - Analysis reports use PTs (standardized terms) - Safety summaries organized by SOC for clarity

26.3 Basic Safety Metrics

26.3.1 Incidence Rate

The incidence is the proportion of subjects experiencing an AE at least once.

\[\text{Incidence Rate} = \frac{\text{Number of subjects with } \geq 1 \text{ AE}}{\text{Total number of subjects}} \times 100\%\]

Example: In a 100-patient trial, 45 patients experience nausea. \[\text{Incidence of nausea} = \frac{45}{100} = 45\%\]

Important: Counts each subject only once, regardless of how many times they experience the event.

26.3.2 Exposure-Adjusted Incidence Rate (EAIR)

Accounts for different treatment durations (critical when some patients stop treatment early).

\[\text{EAIR} = \frac{\text{Total number of AE events}}{\text{Total exposure time (subject-months)}} \times 100\]

Example: - Trial: 100 patients, 200 adverse events total - Average exposure: 0.8 years per subject - Total person-years: \(100 \times 0.8 = 80\) person-years \[\text{EAIR} = \frac{200}{80} = 2.5 \text{ events per person-year}\]

Clinical interpretation: On average, each patient experiences 2.5 adverse events per year of treatment. Allows fair comparison between treatment arms with different exposure patterns.

26.3.3 Event Rate (by Visit)

Tracks incidence across time periods to identify peak risk windows.

\[\text{Event rate at visit } j = \frac{\text{Subjects with AE at visit } j}{\text{Subjects at risk at visit } j} \times 100\%\]

26.4 Safety Summary Tables

A comprehensive AE summary table is the foundation of any safety report.

Preferred Term              Drug (N=100)    Placebo (N=100)
                              n (%)           n (%)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Nervous System Disorders      35 (35%)        20 (20%)
  Headache                    15 (15%)        8 (8%)
  Dizziness                   12 (12%)        7 (7%)
  Tremor                      8 (8%)          5 (5%)

  Nausea                      28 (28%)        8 (8%)
  Vomiting                    10 (10%)        5 (5%)
  Diarrhea                    4 (4%)          2 (2%)

26.4.2 Grade (Severity) Classification

Most trials use CTCAE (Common Terminology Criteria for Adverse Events) grades:

  • Grade 1 (Mild): Asymptomatic or mild symptoms; no intervention needed
  • Grade 2 (Moderate): Minimal, local or non-invasive intervention indicated
  • Grade 3 (Severe): Severe or medically significant but not life-threatening
  • Grade 4 (Life-threatening): Life-threatening; urgent intervention needed
  • Grade 5 (Fatal): Death related to AE

Example: - Grade 1 nausea: “Occasional nausea, managed with antacids” - Grade 2 nausea: “Frequent nausea affecting daily activities, requires medication” - Grade 3 nausea: “Severe; parenteral nutrition required”

26.4.3 Maximum Grade per Subject

Report the worst grade experienced by each subject, not total count.

Logic: If a patient experienced Grade 1 nausea on Day 5 and Grade 3 nausea on Day 12, report Grade 3 as their maximum grade.

# Compute maximum grade per subject per PT
ae_max_grade <- ae_data %>%
  group_by(USUBJID, ARM, AEDECOD) %>%
  summarise(
    max_grade = max(AESEV, na.rm = TRUE),
    .groups = "drop"
  ) %>%
  group_by(AEDECOD, ARM, max_grade) %>%
  summarise(
    n = n_distinct(USUBJID),
    .groups = "drop"
  ) %>%
  pivot_wider(
    names_from = max_grade,
    values_from = n,
    values_fill = 0
  )

26.4.4 Relationship to Study Drug

Classify each AE by investigator assessment:

  • Related: Reasonable possibility of causal relationship to drug
  • Possibly related: Temporal association but other causes possible
  • Not related: Clear alternative etiology (e.g., viral infection)

Clinical use: Safety signals typically focus on related/possibly related AEs.

26.4.5 Action Taken

Track whether AE led to:

  • No action: Continue treatment at same dose
  • Dose reduction: Lower dose due to tolerability
  • Dose interruption: Temporary pause, then resume
  • Discontinuation: Permanent cessation of treatment
  • Other: Hospitalization, emergency visit, etc.

26.5 Publication-Ready Safety Tables

26.5.1 Using gtsummary

library(pharmaversesdtm)
library(gtsummary)
library(tidyverse)

data(ae)

# Prepare AE data
ae_summary <- ae %>%
  filter(AESER == "N") %>%  # Non-serious AEs
  select(USUBJID, ARM, AESOC, AEDECOD, AEGRAD = AEGRADE) %>%
  distinct() %>%  # One row per subject per PT
  group_by(AEDECOD, AESOC, ARM) %>%
  summarise(
    n = n_distinct(USUBJID),
    .groups = "drop"
  ) %>%
  arrange(AESOC, AEDECOD)

# Count total subjects by treatment
n_subjects <- ae %>%
  group_by(ARM) %>%
  summarise(N = n_distinct(USUBJID), .groups = "drop")

# Create summary table
ae_table <- ae_summary %>%
  left_join(n_subjects, by = "ARM") %>%
  mutate(
    Pct = 100 * n / N,
    `n (%)` = sprintf("%d (%.1f%%)", n, Pct)
  ) %>%
  select(AESOC, AEDECOD, ARM, `n (%)`) %>%
  pivot_wider(
    names_from = ARM,
    values_from = `n (%)`
  ) %>%
  arrange(AESOC, AEDECOD)

print(ae_table)

# Using gtsummary for formatted output
ae_gt <- ae_summary %>%
  tbl_summary(
    by = ARM,
    type = list(AEDECOD ~ "categorical"),
    label = list(AEDECOD ~ "Adverse Event")

  modify_header(label ~ "System Organ Class / Preferred Term")

ae_gt

26.6 Time-to-Onset Analysis

Understanding when AEs typically occur guides monitoring strategies.

26.6.1 Kaplan-Meier Curves for AE Onset

library(survival)
library(ggsurvfit)

# Prepare data: time to first occurrence of specific AE
ae_first <- ae %>%
  filter(AEDECOD == "Headache") %>%
  group_by(USUBJID, ARM) %>%
  slice(1) %>%  # First occurrence only
  ungroup() %>%
  select(USUBJID, ARM, AESTDY) %>%
  distinct()

# Add censoring for subjects without the AE
ae_censored <- ae %>%
  select(USUBJID, ARM) %>%
  distinct() %>%
  anti_join(ae_first, by = "USUBJID") %>%
  mutate(
    AESTDY = 365,  # Study duration (days)
    event = 0
  )

ae_first <- ae_first %>%
  mutate(event = 1) %>%
  bind_rows(ae_censored)

# Fit Kaplan-Meier for AE onset
km_ae <- survfit(
  Surv(AESTDY, event) ~ ARM,
  data = ae_first
)

# Plot
if (requireNamespace("ggsurvfit", quietly = TRUE)) {
  gg_ae <- ggsurvfit::ggsurvfit(km_ae) +
    labs(
      title = "Time to Onset of Headache",
      x = "Days from Treatment Start",
      y = "Probability of Remaining Free from Headache",
      caption = "Kaplan-Meier curve for first occurrence"
    ) +
    theme_minimal() +
    theme(
      legend.position = "inside",
      legend.position.inside = c(0.75, 0.25)
    )

  print(gg_ae)
}

# Median time to onset
summary(km_ae, times = c(30, 60, 90))
# Shows probability of remaining AE-free at key time points

26.7 Laboratory Shift Tables

Shift tables track changes in lab values from baseline to post-treatment (Normal → Abnormal).

26.7.1 Structure

                           Post-Treatment Status
                    Normal      Low       High      Total
Baseline Status   ───────────────────────────────────────
Normal              45         5         2         52
Low                  3        10         2         15
High                 1         1        30         32
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Total               49        16        34         99

Interpretation: - Diagonal (Normal → Normal, High → High): No change - Off-diagonal: Shifts toward abnormality require investigation - Example: 5 subjects with normal baseline shifted to low (e.g., hemoglobin decline)

26.7.2 Implementation

# Prepare baseline and final visit lab values
lb_alt <- lb %>%
  filter(LBTESTCD == "ALT", !is.na(LBNRIND)) %>%
  select(USUBJID, VISITNUM, LBNRIND) %>%
  arrange(USUBJID, VISITNUM)

lb_baseline <- lb_alt %>%
  group_by(USUBJID) %>%
  slice_min(VISITNUM, n = 1, with_ties = FALSE) %>%
  ungroup() %>%
  select(USUBJID, Baseline = LBNRIND)

lb_final <- lb_alt %>%
  group_by(USUBJID) %>%
  slice_max(VISITNUM, n = 1, with_ties = FALSE) %>%
  ungroup() %>%
  select(USUBJID, Final = LBNRIND)

lb_shift <- lb_baseline %>%
  inner_join(lb_final, by = "USUBJID") %>%
  filter(!(is.na(Baseline) | is.na(Final))) %>%
  mutate(
    Baseline = factor(Baseline, levels = c("N", "L", "H"),
                      labels = c("Normal", "Low", "High")),
    Final    = factor(Final,    levels = c("N", "L", "H"),
                      labels = c("Normal", "Low", "High"))
  )

# Count transitions
shift_table <- lb_shift %>%
  group_by(Baseline, Final) %>%
  summarise(n = n_distinct(USUBJID), .groups = "drop") %>%
  pivot_wider(names_from = Final, values_from = n, values_fill = 0)

print(shift_table)

# Create shift plot with ggplot
shift_plot <- lb_shift %>%
  count(Baseline, Final) %>%
  ggplot(aes(x = Baseline, y = Final, fill = n)) +
  geom_tile() +
  geom_text(aes(label = n), color = "white", size = 5) +
  scale_fill_viridis_c() +
  labs(
    title = "Laboratory Value Shift Table: Hemoglobin",
    x = "Baseline Status",
    y = "Final Visit Status",
    fill = "Number of Subjects"
  ) +
  theme_minimal()

print(shift_plot)

26.8 Maximum Grade Analysis

Summarize AE severity distribution per PT and treatment.

# Compute maximum grade per subject per PT
ae_max <- ae %>%
  left_join(dm %>% filter(!is.na(ARM), ARM != "") %>% select(USUBJID, ARM), by = "USUBJID") %>%
  mutate(
    grade_num = case_match(AESEV,
      "MILD" ~ 1L, "MODERATE" ~ 2L, "SEVERE" ~ 3L,
      .default = NA_integer_)
  ) %>%
  group_by(USUBJID, ARM, AEDECOD) %>%
  summarise(
    max_grade = max(grade_num, na.rm = TRUE),
    .groups = "drop"
  ) %>%
  filter(is.finite(max_grade))

# Create table: PT x Grade distribution
grade_table <- ae_max %>%
  count(AEDECOD, ARM, max_grade) %>%
  pivot_wider(
    names_from = max_grade,
    values_from = n,
    names_prefix = "Grade_",
    values_fill = 0
  ) %>%
  { df <- .; for (g in c("Grade_3","Grade_4")) if (!g %in% names(df)) df[[g]] <- 0L; df } %>%
  arrange(desc(Grade_3 + Grade_4))  # Sort by severe AEs

print(grade_table)

# Visualization: Stacked bar chart
ae_grade_plot <- ae_max %>%
  count(ARM, AEDECOD, max_grade) %>%
  filter(n > 0) %>%
  arrange(AEDECOD, max_grade) %>%
  ggplot(aes(x = reorder(AEDECOD, -n), y = n, fill = factor(max_grade))) +
  geom_col(position = "stack") +
  facet_wrap(~ARM) +
  scale_fill_brewer(palette = "RdYlGn_r", name = "Grade") +
  labs(
    title = "Distribution of Adverse Event Severity",
    x = "Preferred Term",
    y = "Number of Subjects"
  ) +
  theme_minimal() +
  theme(axis.text.x = element_text(angle = 45, hjust = 1))

print(ae_grade_plot)

26.9 Serious Adverse Events (SAEs) and Discontinuations

Critical safety analyses focus on high-impact events.

26.9.1 SAE Summary

# SAE incidence by treatment
sae_summary <- ae %>%
  filter(AESER == "Y") %>%  # Serious AEs only
  group_by(ARM) %>%
  summarise(
    n_subjects = n_distinct(USUBJID),
    n_events = n(),
    .groups = "drop"
  ) %>%
  left_join(
    ae %>% select(USUBJID, ARM) %>% distinct() %>%
      group_by(ARM) %>%
      summarise(N_total = n_distinct(USUBJID), .groups = "drop"),
    by = "ARM"
  ) %>%
  mutate(
    pct_subjects = 100 * n_subjects / N_total,
    event_rate = n_events / N_total
  )

print(sae_summary)

# List all SAEs with details
sae_list <- ae %>%
  filter(AESER == "Y") %>%
  select(any_of(c("USUBJID", "ARM", "AEDECOD", "AETOXGR", "AEACN", "AEREL"))) %>%
  arrange(USUBJID)

print(sae_list)

26.9.2 Discontinuation Due to AE

# Count discontinuations by treatment
disc_summary <- ae %>%
  filter(AEACN %in% c("DRUG DISCONTINUED", "DOSE REDUCED")) %>%
  group_by(USUBJID, ARM) %>%
  slice(1) %>%  # First such event per subject
  ungroup() %>%
  group_by(ARM) %>%
  summarise(
    n_discontinued = n_distinct(USUBJID),
    .groups = "drop"
  ) %>%
  left_join(
    ae %>% select(USUBJID, ARM) %>% distinct() %>%
      group_by(ARM) %>%
      summarise(N_total = n_distinct(USUBJID), .groups = "drop"),
    by = "ARM"
  ) %>%
  mutate(
    pct_discontinued = 100 * n_discontinued / N_total,
    `n (%)` = sprintf("%d (%.1f%%)", n_discontinued, pct_discontinued)
  )

print(disc_summary %>% select(ARM, `n (%)`))

# Compute AE incidence by PT and treatment
ae_incidence <- ae %>%

    fill = "Higher in Arm"
  ) +
  theme_minimal()

print(ae_comparison)

26.11 Clinical Example: Comprehensive Safety Analysis

library(pharmaversesdtm)
library(admiral)
library(gtsummary)
library(tidyverse)

# Load datasets
data(ae)
data(ex)  # Exposure for calculating EAIR

# 1. Basic AE incidence
ae_summary <- ae %>%
  filter(AESER == "N") %>%
  select(USUBJID, ARM, AEDECOD, AEGRADE) %>%
  distinct() %>%
  group_by(AEDECOD, ARM) %>%
  summarise(
    n = n_distinct(USUBJID),
    .groups = "drop"
  ) %>%
  left_join(
    ae %>% select(USUBJID, ARM) %>% distinct() %>%
      group_by(ARM) %>%
      summarise(N = n_distinct(USUBJID), .groups = "drop"),
    by = "ARM"
  ) %>%
  mutate(`n (%)` = sprintf("%d (%.1f%%)", n, 100 * n / N)) %>%
  select(AEDECOD, ARM, `n (%)`) %>%
  pivot_wider(names_from = ARM, values_from = `n (%)`) %>%
  arrange(desc(AEDECOD))

print(ae_summary)

# 2. Calculate EAIR
exposure <- ex %>%
  group_by(USUBJID, ARM) %>%
  summarise(
    exposure_days = max(EXENDY, na.rm = TRUE),
    exposure_years = exposure_days / 365.25,
    .groups = "drop"
  )

ae_events <- ae %>%
  group_by(ARM) %>%
  summarise(total_ae_events = n(), .groups = "drop")

eair_calc <- exposure %>%
  group_by(ARM) %>%
  summarise(
    total_exposure_years = sum(exposure_years, na.rm = TRUE),
    n_subjects = n_distinct(USUBJID),
    .groups = "drop"
  ) %>%
  left_join(ae_events, by = "ARM") %>%
  mutate(
    EAIR = (total_ae_events / total_exposure_years) * 100,
    EAIR_text = sprintf("%.2f per 100 person-years", EAIR)
  )

print(eair_calc %>% select(ARM, EAIR_text))

# 3. SAE summary
sae_summary <- ae %>%
  filter(AESER == "Y") %>%
  group_by(ARM) %>%
  summarise(
    n_sae_subjects = n_distinct(USUBJID),
    n_sae_events = n(),
    .groups = "drop"
  ) %>%
  left_join(
    ae %>% select(USUBJID, ARM) %>% distinct() %>%
      group_by(ARM) %>%
      summarise(N_total = n_distinct(USUBJID), .groups = "drop"),
    by = "ARM"
  ) %>%
  mutate(
    `SAE subjects n (%)` = sprintf("%d (%.1f%%)", n_sae_subjects,
                                     100 * n_sae_subjects / N_total),
    `SAE rate` = sprintf("%.2f per 100 subjects", 100 * n_sae_events / N_total)
  ) %>%
  select(ARM, `SAE subjects n (%)`, `SAE rate`)

print(sae_summary)

# 4. Discontinuation due to AE
disc_ae <- ae %>%
  filter(AEACN == "DRUG DISCONTINUED") %>%
  group_by(USUBJID, ARM) %>%
  slice(1) %>%
  ungroup()

disc_summary <- ae %>%
  select(USUBJID, ARM) %>%
  distinct() %>%
  group_by(ARM) %>%
  summarise(N = n_distinct(USUBJID), .groups = "drop") %>%

# Safety Analysis in Clinical Trials

## Introduction: Why Safety Analysis Matters

While efficacy demonstrates that a treatment **works**, safety analysis documents how well patients **tolerate** it. Regulatory agencies (FDA, EMA) require comprehensive safety assessments before drug approval. Safety findings can limit patient populations, require monitoring strategies, or prevent approval entirely.

### Key Safety Endpoints

- **Adverse Events (AEs)**: Any undesired medical occurrence (rash, nausea, elevated liver enzymes)
- **Serious Adverse Events (SAEs)**: Life-threatening, result in hospitalization, disability, or death
- **Adverse Events of Special Interest (AESIs)**: Clinically important events predetermined as requiring careful monitoring (e.g., myocardial infarction in cardiovascular trials)
- **Dose Limiting Toxicity (DLT)**: Event requiring dose reduction or discontinuation
- **Discontinuation**: Patient stops treatment due to adverse event

## MedDRA Coding and Hierarchy

**Medical Dictionary for Regulatory Activities (MedDRA)** is the standard for coding clinical safety terms globally.

### MedDRA Structure

The hierarchy from general to specific:

1. **System Organ Class (SOC)**: Highest level (e.g., "Nervous System Disorders", "Gastrointestinal Disorders")
2. **High Level Group Term (HLGT)**: Groups related preferred terms
3. **High Level Term (HLT)**: Intermediate grouping
4. **Preferred Term (PT)**: Standardized term used in reporting (e.g., "Headache", "Nausea")
5. **Lowest Level Term (LLT)**: Actual patient-reported symptom (e.g., "Bad headache", "Feeling sick")

### Example Hierarchy

```
SOC: Nervous System Disorders
  HLGT: Headaches
    HLT: Headaches (excl migraine)
      PT: Headache
        LLT: Bad headache, Severe headache, Headache worse
```

**Clinical Practice**:
- Data collection typically uses LLTs (patient language)
- Analysis reports use PTs (standardized terms)
- Safety summaries organized by SOC for clarity

## Basic Safety Metrics

### Incidence Rate

The **incidence** is the proportion of subjects experiencing an AE at least once.

$$\text{Incidence Rate} = \frac{\text{Number of subjects with } \geq 1 \text{ AE}}{\text{Total number of subjects}} \times 100\%$$

**Example**: In a 100-patient trial, 45 patients experience nausea.
$$\text{Incidence of nausea} = \frac{45}{100} = 45\%$$

**Important**: Counts each subject only once, regardless of how many times they experience the event.

### Exposure-Adjusted Incidence Rate (EAIR)

Accounts for different treatment durations (critical when some patients stop treatment early).

$$\text{EAIR} = \frac{\text{Total number of AE events}}{\text{Total exposure time (subject-months)}} \times 100$$

**Example**:
- Trial: 100 patients, 200 adverse events total
- Average exposure: 0.8 years per subject
- Total person-years: $100 \times 0.8 = 80$ person-years
$$\text{EAIR} = \frac{200}{80} = 2.5 \text{ events per person-year}$$

**Clinical interpretation**: On average, each patient experiences 2.5 adverse events per year of treatment. Allows fair comparison between treatment arms with different exposure patterns.

### Event Rate (by Visit)

Tracks incidence across time periods to identify peak risk windows.

$$\text{Event rate at visit } j = \frac{\text{Subjects with AE at visit } j}{\text{Subjects at risk at visit } j} \times 100\%$$

### Grade (Severity) Classification

Most trials use **CTCAE (Common Terminology Criteria for Adverse Events)** grades:

- **Grade 1 (Mild)**: Asymptomatic or mild symptoms; no intervention needed
- **Grade 2 (Moderate)**: Minimal, local or non-invasive intervention indicated
- **Grade 3 (Severe)**: Severe or medically significant but not life-threatening
- **Grade 4 (Life-threatening)**: Life-threatening; urgent intervention needed
- **Grade 5 (Fatal)**: Death related to AE

**Example**:
- Grade 1 nausea: "Occasional nausea, managed with antacids"
- Grade 2 nausea: "Frequent nausea affecting daily activities, requires medication"
- Grade 3 nausea: "Severe; parenteral nutrition required"

### Maximum Grade per Subject

Report the **worst grade** experienced by each subject, not total count.

**Logic**: If a patient experienced Grade 1 nausea on Day 5 and Grade 3 nausea on Day 12, report Grade 3 as their maximum grade.

### Relationship to Study Drug

Classify each AE by investigator assessment:

- **Related**: Reasonable possibility of causal relationship to drug
- **Possibly related**: Temporal association but other causes possible
- **Not related**: Clear alternative etiology (e.g., viral infection)

**Clinical use**: Safety signals typically focus on related/possibly related AEs.

### Action Taken

Track whether AE led to:

- **No action**: Continue treatment at same dose
- **Dose reduction**: Lower dose due to tolerability
- **Dose interruption**: Temporary pause, then resume
- **Discontinuation**: Permanent cessation of treatment
- **Other**: Hospitalization, emergency visit, etc.

## Laboratory Shift Tables

Shift tables track changes in lab values from baseline to post-treatment (Normal → Abnormal).

### Structure

```
                           Post-Treatment Status
                    Normal      Low       High      Total
Baseline Status   ───────────────────────────────────────
Normal              45         5         2         52
Low                  3        10         2         15
High                 1         1        30         32
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Total               49        16        34         99
```

**Interpretation**:
- Diagonal (Normal → Normal, High → High): No change
- Off-diagonal: Shifts toward abnormality require investigation
- Example: 5 subjects with normal baseline shifted to low (e.g., hemoglobin decline)

### Implementation

```r
# Prepare baseline and final visit lab values
lb_alt <- lb %>%
  filter(LBTESTCD == "ALT", !is.na(LBNRIND)) %>%
  select(USUBJID, VISITNUM, LBNRIND) %>%
  arrange(USUBJID, VISITNUM)

lb_baseline <- lb_alt %>%
  group_by(USUBJID) %>%
  slice_min(VISITNUM, n = 1, with_ties = FALSE) %>%
  ungroup() %>%
  select(USUBJID, Baseline = LBNRIND)

lb_final <- lb_alt %>%
  group_by(USUBJID) %>%
  slice_max(VISITNUM, n = 1, with_ties = FALSE) %>%

lb_shift <- lb_baseline %>%
  inner_join(lb_final, by = "USUBJID") %>%
```

**Exercise 15.1**: Load pharmaversesdtm::ae data. Create a publication-ready AE summary table showing the incidence of all AEs by SOC and PT, stratified by treatment arm. Format using `gt` or `gtsummary`.

**Exercise 15.2**: Calculate and report the Exposure-Adjusted Incidence Rate (EAIR) for AEs in each treatment arm using exposure data from `pharmaversesdtm::ex`. Interpret the EAIR values clinically.

**Exercise 15.3**: Create a shift table for laboratory values (e.g., hemoglobin: Normal → Low → High) from baseline to final visit. Discuss which shifts warrant clinical concern.

### Advanced Exercises

**Exercise 15.4**: Implement a **time-to-onset analysis** for a specific AE (e.g., headache). Use Kaplan-Meier curves to compare onset timing between treatment arms. Estimate median time to onset and create a publication-quality plot.

**Exercise 15.5**: Conduct a **safety signal detection** analysis. Using the AE data, identify AEs where the incidence difference between treatment arms exceeds 5 percentage points. For each signal, stratify by patient subgroup (age, baseline risk) to assess consistency. Discuss which signals require further investigation or potential labeling.
