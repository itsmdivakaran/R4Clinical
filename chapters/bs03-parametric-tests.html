12  Parametric Statistical Tests in Clinical Trials

12.1 Introduction

Parametric tests are the workhorses of clinical trial statistics. They assume that the data follow a known probability distribution — most commonly the normal distribution — and use the parameters of that distribution (mean, variance) to draw inferences. When these assumptions hold, parametric tests are the most powerful tools available for detecting true treatment effects.

This chapter covers the full spectrum of parametric tests used in clinical development:

  • t-tests: One-sample, two-sample independent, and paired designs
  • ANOVA: One-way and two-way analysis of variance across treatment arms
  • ANCOVA: Covariate adjustment for baseline measurements
  • Assumption checking: Normality tests, homogeneity of variance, and diagnostic plots
  • Post-hoc tests: Multiple comparison procedures after ANOVA

All examples use the pharmaversesdtm dataset, which provides realistic CDISC-formatted clinical trial data.

12.3 1. One-Sample t-Test

12.3.1 1.1 Theory

The one-sample t-test asks: does the population mean differ from a specified value (mu)?

\[t = \frac{\bar{x} - \mu_0}{s / \sqrt{n}}\]

where \(\bar{x}\) is the sample mean, \(\mu_0\) is the hypothesised value, \(s\) is the sample standard deviation, and \(n\) is the sample size. The test statistic follows a \(t\)-distribution with \(n - 1\) degrees of freedom under \(H_0\).

Hypotheses:

  • \(H_0: \mu = \mu_0\) (population mean equals the hypothesised value)
  • \(H_1: \mu \neq \mu_0\) (two-tailed)

Clinical question: Is the mean age of participants in this trial different from 65 years — the typical cut-off used for elderly subgroup analyses?

12.4 2. Two-Sample Independent t-Test

12.4.1 2.1 Theory

The two-sample independent t-test compares the means of two unrelated groups:

\[t = \frac{\bar{x}_1 - \bar{x}_2}{\sqrt{s_p^2 \left(\frac{1}{n_1} + \frac{1}{n_2}\right)}}\]

where \(s_p^2\) is the pooled variance (assuming equal variances). When variances are unequal, Welch’s correction adjusts the degrees of freedom.

Clinical question: Does the mean age differ between the Placebo and Xanomeline High Dose arms? (Age balance at baseline validates randomisation.)

12.4.2 2.2 Filter to Two Arms

# Filter to exactly two arms for comparison

12.5 3. Paired t-Test

12.5.1 3.1 Theory

The paired t-test compares two related measurements — typically the same subject at two time points:

\[t = \frac{\bar{d}}{s_d / \sqrt{n}}\]

where \(\bar{d}\) is the mean of within-subject differences and \(s_d\) is their standard deviation. This design removes between-subject variability, yielding greater power than the two-sample test.

Clinical question: Does systolic blood pressure (SYSBP) change significantly from screening (VISITNUM = 1) to the first follow-up visit (VISITNUM = 2)?

12.5.2 3.2 Data Preparation

# Extract baseline (VISITNUM=1) and first follow-up (VISITNUM=2) SYSBP
vs_paired <- vs_sysbp |>
  filter(VISITNUM %in% c(1, 2)) |>
  select(USUBJID, VISITNUM, VSSTRESN) |>
  group_by(USUBJID, VISITNUM) |>
  summarise(VSSTRESN = mean(VSSTRESN, na.rm = TRUE), .groups = "drop") |>
  tidyr::pivot_wider(names_from  = VISITNUM,
                     values_from = VSSTRESN,
                     names_prefix = "VISIT") |>
  filter(!is.na(VISIT1), !is.na(VISIT2)) |>
  mutate(diff = VISIT2 - VISIT1)

cat("Subjects with both VISITNUM 1 and 2:", nrow(vs_paired), "\n")
#> Subjects with both VISITNUM 1 and 2: 250
cat("Mean difference (Visit2 - Visit1):", round(mean(vs_paired$diff), 2), "mmHg\n")
#> Mean difference (Visit2 - Visit1): -3.93 mmHg
cat("SD of differences:", round(sd(vs_paired$diff), 2), "mmHg\n")
#> SD of differences: 13.03 mmHg

#> Shapiro-Wilk on differences: W = 0.987 , p = 0.0229

result_paired <- t.test(vs_paired$VISIT2, vs_paired$VISIT1,

#> 
#>  Paired t-test
#> 
#> data:  vs_paired$VISIT2 and vs_paired$VISIT1
#> t = -4.7712, df = 249, p-value = 3.122e-06
#> alternative hypothesis: true mean difference is not equal to 0

#>  -5.556986 -2.309681

#> mean difference

#> --- Clinical Interpretation ---

#> Mean SBP at Visit 1: 139.4 mmHg
cat("Mean SBP at Visit 2:", round(mean(vs_paired$VISIT2), 1), "mmHg\n")

#> Mean change: -3.9 mmHg

12.6 4. One-Way ANOVA

12.6.1 4.1 Theory

One-way ANOVA extends the two-sample t-test to \(k \geq 3\) groups. It partitions total variability into:

\[\text{SS}_{\text{Total}} = \text{SS}_{\text{Between}} + \text{SS}_{\text{Within}}\]

The F-statistic:

\[F = \frac{\text{MS}_{\text{Between}}}{\text{MS}_{\text{Within}}} = \frac{\text{SS}_{\text{Between}} / (k-1)}{\text{SS}_{\text{Within}} / (N-k)}\]

Clinical question: Does mean systolic blood pressure differ across the three treatment arms?

12.6.2 4.2 Assumption Checks

# Use Visit 1 (baseline) SBP across all 3 arms
vs_anova <- vs_sysbp |>
  filter(VISITNUM == 1, !is.na(VSSTRESN)) |>
  select(USUBJID, ARM, VSSTRESN)

cat("Sample sizes by ARM:\n")
#> Sample sizes by ARM:
print(table(vs_anova$ARM))
#> 
#>              Placebo  Xanomeline Low Dose Xanomeline High Dose 
#>                  258                  252                  251
# Shapiro-Wilk per group
cat("\nShapiro-Wilk normality tests by ARM:\n")
#> 
#> Shapiro-Wilk normality tests by ARM:
by(vs_anova$VSSTRESN, vs_anova$ARM, function(x) {
  sw <- shapiro.test(x)
  cat("  W =", round(sw$statistic, 4), ", p =", round(sw$p.value, 4), "\n")
})

12.7 5. Two-Way ANOVA: Treatment × Sex Interaction

12.7.1 5.1 Theory

Two-way ANOVA simultaneously tests the effects of two categorical factors and their interaction:

\[y_{ijk} = \mu + \alpha_i + \beta_j + (\alpha\beta)_{ij} + \epsilon_{ijk}\]

12.8 6. ANCOVA: Adjusting for Baseline

12.8.1 6.1 Theory

ANCOVA (Analysis of Covariance) combines ANOVA with linear regression. It removes the variability explained by a covariate (e.g., baseline measurement) before testing for group differences:

\[y_{ij} = \mu + \tau_i + \beta(x_{ij} - \bar{x}) + \epsilon_{ij}\]

where \(\tau_i\) is the treatment effect and \(\beta\) is the regression coefficient for the baseline covariate \(x\).

Benefits in clinical trials: - Increased statistical power (reduced residual variance) - Control for baseline imbalances even after randomisation - Required by ICH E9(R1) for many primary efficacy analyses

Clinical question: Does treatment arm affect SBP at Visit 2 after adjusting for baseline (Visit 1) SBP?

12.10 8. Post-Hoc Tests: TukeyHSD

12.10.1 8.1 Why Post-Hoc Tests?

When ANOVA rejects \(H_0\), we know at least one group differs — but not which pairs. Post-hoc procedures make all pairwise comparisons while controlling the family-wise error rate (FWER).

Tukey’s Honestly Significant Difference (HSD): Controls FWER at \(\alpha\) for all \(\binom{k}{2}\) pairwise comparisons. Appropriate when comparing all pairs with equal sample sizes.

# Parametric Statistical Tests in Clinical Trials

## Introduction

Parametric tests are the workhorses of clinical trial statistics. They assume that the data follow a known probability distribution — most commonly the normal distribution — and use the parameters of that distribution (mean, variance) to draw inferences. When these assumptions hold, parametric tests are the most powerful tools available for detecting true treatment effects.

This chapter covers the full spectrum of parametric tests used in clinical development:

- **t-tests**: One-sample, two-sample independent, and paired designs
- **ANOVA**: One-way and two-way analysis of variance across treatment arms
- **ANCOVA**: Covariate adjustment for baseline measurements
- **Assumption checking**: Normality tests, homogeneity of variance, and diagnostic plots
- **Post-hoc tests**: Multiple comparison procedures after ANOVA

All examples use the `pharmaversesdtm` dataset, which provides realistic CDISC-formatted clinical trial data.

## 1. One-Sample t-Test

### 1.1 Theory

The one-sample t-test asks: **does the population mean differ from a specified value (mu)?**

$$t = \frac{\bar{x} - \mu_0}{s / \sqrt{n}}$$

where $\bar{x}$ is the sample mean, $\mu_0$ is the hypothesised value, $s$ is the sample standard deviation, and $n$ is the sample size. The test statistic follows a $t$-distribution with $n - 1$ degrees of freedom under $H_0$.

**Hypotheses:**

- $H_0: \mu = \mu_0$ (population mean equals the hypothesised value)
- $H_1: \mu \neq \mu_0$ (two-tailed)

**Clinical question:** Is the mean age of participants in this trial different from 65 years — the typical cut-off used for elderly subgroup analyses?

## 2. Two-Sample Independent t-Test

### 2.1 Theory

The two-sample independent t-test compares the means of two unrelated groups:

$$t = \frac{\bar{x}_1 - \bar{x}_2}{\sqrt{s_p^2 \left(\frac{1}{n_1} + \frac{1}{n_2}\right)}}$$

where $s_p^2$ is the pooled variance (assuming equal variances). When variances are unequal, Welch's correction adjusts the degrees of freedom.

## 3. Paired t-Test

### 3.1 Theory

The paired t-test compares two related measurements — typically the same subject at two time points:

$$t = \frac{\bar{d}}{s_d / \sqrt{n}}$$

where $\bar{d}$ is the mean of within-subject differences and $s_d$ is their standard deviation. This design removes between-subject variability, yielding greater power than the two-sample test.

**Clinical question:** Does systolic blood pressure (SYSBP) change significantly from screening (VISITNUM = 1) to the first follow-up visit (VISITNUM = 2)?

## 4. One-Way ANOVA

### 4.1 Theory

One-way ANOVA extends the two-sample t-test to $k \geq 3$ groups. It partitions total variability into:

$$\text{SS}_{\text{Total}} = \text{SS}_{\text{Between}} + \text{SS}_{\text{Within}}$$

The F-statistic:

$$F = \frac{\text{MS}_{\text{Between}}}{\text{MS}_{\text{Within}}} = \frac{\text{SS}_{\text{Between}} / (k-1)}{\text{SS}_{\text{Within}} / (N-k)}$$

**Clinical question:** Does mean systolic blood pressure differ across the three treatment arms?

## 5. Two-Way ANOVA: Treatment × Sex Interaction

### 5.1 Theory

Two-way ANOVA simultaneously tests the effects of two categorical factors and their interaction:

$$y_{ijk} = \mu + \alpha_i + \beta_j + (\alpha\beta)_{ij} + \epsilon_{ijk}$$

where $\alpha_i$ = ARM effect, $\beta_j$ = SEX effect, $(\alpha\beta)_{ij}$ = interaction. A significant interaction means the effect of treatment differs by sex (or vice versa) — critical for subgroup analyses in regulatory submissions.

**Clinical question:** Does the treatment arm effect on SBP differ by sex?

## 6. ANCOVA: Adjusting for Baseline

### 6.1 Theory

ANCOVA (Analysis of Covariance) combines ANOVA with linear regression. It removes the variability explained by a covariate (e.g., baseline measurement) before testing for group differences:

$$y_{ij} = \mu + \tau_i + \beta(x_{ij} - \bar{x}) + \epsilon_{ij}$$

where $\tau_i$ is the treatment effect and $\beta$ is the regression coefficient for the baseline covariate $x$.

**Benefits in clinical trials:**
- Increased statistical power (reduced residual variance)
- Control for baseline imbalances even after randomisation
- Required by ICH E9(R1) for many primary efficacy analyses

**Clinical question:** Does treatment arm affect SBP at Visit 2 after adjusting for baseline (Visit 1) SBP?

### 6.2 Fitting ANCOVA

```{r}
#| label: ancova-fit

# Build dataset: baseline (V1) and post-treatment (V2) SBP with ARM
vs_ancova <- vs_sysbp |>
  filter(VISITNUM %in% c(1, 2)) |>
  select(USUBJID, VISITNUM, VSSTRESN, ARM) |>
  group_by(USUBJID, VISITNUM, ARM) |>
  summarise(VSSTRESN = mean(VSSTRESN, na.rm = TRUE), .groups = "drop") |>
  tidyr::pivot_wider(names_from  = VISITNUM,
                     values_from = VSSTRESN,
                     names_prefix = "V") |>
  filter(!is.na(V1), !is.na(V2))

cat("ANCOVA dataset: n =", nrow(vs_ancova), "\n")
cat("Missingness check: V1 missing =", sum(is.na(vs_ancova$V1)),
    ", V2 missing =", sum(is.na(vs_ancova$V2)), "\n\n")

# ANCOVA model: post-treatment SBP ~ ARM + baseline SBP
model_ancova <- lm(V2 ~ ARM + V1, data = vs_ancova)
ancova_anova <- car::Anova(model_ancova, type = "III")
print(ancova_anova)

cat("\n--- Coefficient Table ---\n")
print(summary(model_ancova)$coefficients)

cat("\n--- Clinical Interpretation ---\n")
p_arm_ancova <- ancova_anova["ARM", "Pr(>F)"]
cat("Treatment ARM effect after adjusting for baseline SBP:\n")
cat("  p-value =", round(p_arm_ancova, 4), "\n")
cat("  R-squared =", round(summary(model_ancova)$r.squared, 3), "\n")
cat("  Baseline SBP coefficient:", round(coef(model_ancova)["V1"], 3), "\n\n")

if (p_arm_ancova < 0.05) {
  cat("Conclusion: Significant treatment effect on SBP after baseline adjustment.\n")
} else {
  cat("Conclusion: No significant treatment effect after baseline adjustment (p =",
      round(p_arm_ancova, 4), ").\n")
}
```

## 8. Post-Hoc Tests: TukeyHSD

### 8.1 Why Post-Hoc Tests?

When ANOVA rejects $H_0$, we know at least one group differs — but not *which* pairs. Post-hoc procedures make all pairwise comparisons while controlling the family-wise error rate (FWER).

**Tukey's Honestly Significant Difference (HSD):** Controls FWER at $\alpha$ for all $\binom{k}{2}$ pairwise comparisons. Appropriate when comparing all pairs with equal sample sizes.

### 8.2 Tukey HSD with Significant ANOVA

```{r}

# TukeyHSD

cat("\nTukey HSD pairwise comparisons:\n")
print(tukey_result)
```

## Summary

| Test | Use Case | Key Assumption | R Function |
|------|----------|---------------|------------|
| One-sample t-test | Mean vs. known value | Normality | `t.test(x, mu=)` |
| Two-sample t-test | Two group means | Normality, (equal var) | `t.test(y ~ grp)` |
| Paired t-test | Before-after / matched | Differences normal | `t.test(x, y, paired=TRUE)` |
| One-way ANOVA | ≥3 group means | Normality, equal var | `aov(y ~ grp)` |
| Two-way ANOVA | 2 factors + interaction | Normality, equal var | `aov(y ~ A * B)` |
| ANCOVA | Groups + covariate | Linearity, normality | `lm(y ~ grp + cov)` |
| Welch's ANOVA | ≥3 groups, unequal var | Normality | `oneway.test(var.equal=FALSE)` |
| TukeyHSD | Post-hoc all pairs | ANOVA significant | `TukeyHSD(aov_obj)` |
